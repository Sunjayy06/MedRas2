REVIEW OF LITERATURE
Postoperative Sore Throat (POST): Overview
Definition and Incidence After Endotracheal Intubation
Postoperative sore throat (POST) is defined as a spontaneously reported complaint of throat pain, scratchiness, or irritation occurring in the period following recovery from general anaesthesia administered via an endotracheal tube. It encompasses a range of upper airway symptoms that may manifest either in isolation or in combination, including rawness or dryness of the pharynx, frank pain on swallowing, hoarseness of voice, and a persistent irritating cough. While the terminology "sore throat" is used broadly in clinical practice, from a research standpoint it refers specifically to any discomfort localised to the throat that arises as a consequence of airway instrumentation rather than surgical pathology or pre-existing disease. The symptom complex typically declares itself within the first hour of extubation and may persist for up to 72 hours in a subset of patients, though in the majority it resolves within the first postoperative day [12]. Its onset, severity, and duration are influenced by a constellation of patient, procedural, and pharmacological variables that have been the subject of extensive investigation over the past four decades.
The incidence of POST following orotracheal intubation is one of the most widely reported yet most variable figures in the anaesthetic literature, ranging from as low as 14.4% to as high as 90% across different study populations. This remarkable variability is not simply a reflection of true epidemiological differences between patient groups but is also substantially attributable to heterogeneity in study design, inconsistency in the tools used to assess throat discomfort, differences in the timing of postoperative evaluation, and the threshold at which the symptom is considered clinically meaningful. When well-designed prospective studies employing standardised grading instruments are examined, the incidence of any grade of POST in patients undergoing elective surgery under general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation converges around 40–50%, positioning it as one of the commonest adverse outcomes of airway management [13]. Moderate-to-severe POST, which is functionally significant and requires analgesic intervention, occurs in approximately 20–30% of these patients, a figure that, when extrapolated to the global volume of surgical procedures performed annually under general anaesthesia, represents an enormous burden of avoidable patient morbidity.
Clinical Significance and Impact on Patient Comfort
The clinical significance of POST is frequently underestimated by anaesthetists and surgeons alike, who may regard it as a minor and self-limiting inconvenience relative to more immediately life-threatening perioperative complications. However, from the patient's perspective, POST is a highly distressing experience. Studies examining patient-ranked postoperative complaints consistently place sore throat among the top five most bothersome symptoms after surgery, alongside postoperative nausea and vomiting, pain at the operative site, shivering, and drowsiness [14]. In some patient-centred surveys, moderate or severe POST has been rated as more distressing than the surgical pain itself, particularly in procedures where the operative site pain is well-controlled with targeted analgesia but no specific prophylaxis against POST has been administered.
The functional consequences of POST extend well beyond subjective discomfort. Odynophagia, or pain on swallowing, can significantly impair the patient's ability to take adequate oral fluids and nutrition in the early postoperative period, which is of particular concern in day-surgery settings where the ability to drink and tolerate oral intake is a prerequisite for safe discharge [15]. In elderly patients and those with comorbidities that already predispose them to dysphagia or nutritional compromise, the additive effect of POST-related odynophagia may substantially prolong hospital stay and delay return to baseline functional status. Hoarseness, which frequently accompanies POST, can have professional implications for patients whose vocations depend on voice quality, including teachers, singers, lawyers, and healthcare professionals, for whom even a temporary impairment of phonation constitutes a meaningful vocational risk. The persistence of throat symptoms into the days following discharge can also generate unnecessary anxiety in patients who may attribute the symptom to a complication of surgery, leading to unplanned healthcare consultations and adding to the indirect costs of perioperative care [16].
Patient satisfaction with anaesthesia is now widely recognised as a legitimate and measurable healthcare outcome, and POST has been identified as a significant independent determinant of dissatisfaction with anaesthetic care in multivariate analyses of patient experience data. In an era in which patient-reported outcome measures (PROMs) are increasingly integrated into healthcare quality metrics, the prevention of POST is not merely a matter of clinical courtesy but a genuine quality indicator that reflects the technical standard and patient-centredness of perioperative care [17]. The growing emphasis on enhanced recovery after surgery (ERAS) pathways, which prioritise early oral intake, rapid mobilisation, and timely discharge, further elevates the importance of POST prevention, since uncontrolled throat symptoms can impede progress along each of these recovery milestones.
Burden in Anaesthesia Practice
From a public health perspective, the burden imposed by POST on anaesthesia practice is substantial. Given that an estimated 313 million surgical procedures are performed globally each year, and that a significant proportion of these involve general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation, even a conservative incidence estimate of 40% translates into tens of millions of patients annually experiencing POST as a direct consequence of airway management [18]. The direct costs associated with POST include the pharmacological management of established symptoms, extended recovery room stays, unplanned hospital admissions following day surgery, and the administrative burden of patient complaints and follow-up consultations. Indirect costs, which are less easily quantified, include lost productivity, caregiver burden, and the psychological impact on patients who experience a procedure-related complication that they were not counselled to anticipate.
Despite the high prevalence and measurable impact of POST, surveys of anaesthetic practice reveal that prophylactic strategies are not consistently applied, and that awareness of evidence-based interventions for POST prevention remains suboptimal among practitioners. Several national and international anaesthetic societies have now published guidance on the prevention and management of POST, and the proliferation of randomised controlled trials examining prophylactic agents over the past two decades reflects the growing recognition of this condition as a legitimate focus for clinical research and quality improvement [19]. The development of effective, safe, and easily administered preventive interventions — particularly those applicable in diverse clinical settings including resource-limited environments — remains an active and clinically important area of investigation within the broader field of perioperative medicine. The present study was conceived within this context, with the aim of contributing high-quality comparative evidence to inform clinical practice in POST prevention.
Etiopathogenesis of POST
Mucosal Trauma During Laryngoscopy and Intubation
The pathogenesis of POST begins at the moment of laryngoscopy and involves a sequence of mechanical, ischaemic, and inflammatory events that unfold across the oropharyngeal and tracheobronchial mucosal surfaces. Direct laryngoscopy, whether performed with a conventional Macintosh or Miller blade or with a videolaryngoscope, requires the application of levering force to the tongue base and epiglottis in order to achieve adequate visualisation of the glottis. This mechanical manoeuvre subjects the posterior pharyngeal wall, the vallecula, the posterior surface of the epiglottis, and the arytenoid region to compressive and shearing forces that inevitably produce some degree of mucosal microtrauma, even in the hands of experienced anaesthetists performing uncomplicated intubations [20]. The arytenoid cartilages are particularly susceptible because of the thinness and relative fragility of the overlying mucosa, the exposed nature of the posterior laryngeal inlet, and the proximity of the mucosa to the hard cartilaginous surface beneath, which provides minimal cushioning against compressive injury.
The passage of the endotracheal tube through the vocal cords and into the trachea adds a further dimension of mechanical trauma. As the tube traverses the glottis, it makes contact with the vocal folds, the subglottic mucosa, and the anterior and posterior walls of the proximal trachea, generating friction-related injury whose severity is determined by the speed of tube advancement, the diameter of the tube relative to the glottic aperture, the rigidity of the tube material, the presence or absence of lubrication, and the angulation of the tube relative to the laryngeal axis [21]. Stylets and introducers, when used, can amplify this trauma if they project beyond the tube tip or if the tube is advanced with excessive force along a suboptimal trajectory. The subglottic and upper tracheal mucosa, which is lined by a pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium resting on a basement membrane with a rich underlying vasculature, is highly sensitive to frictional injury, and even minor disruptions to epithelial continuity are sufficient to initiate a local inflammatory cascade [22].
Role of Cuff Pressure, Tube Size, and Duration of Intubation
Once the endotracheal tube is in place, the inflated cuff becomes the primary ongoing source of mucosal injury and is the most important modifiable determinant of POST severity. The cuff, when inflated within the tracheal lumen, must generate a radial pressure against the tracheal wall sufficient to achieve an adequate seal for ventilation. However, this sealing pressure comes at the cost of compression of the tracheal mucosa, and when the intraluminal cuff pressure exceeds the mucosal capillary perfusion pressure — generally estimated at 20–30 cmH₂O — blood flow to the compressed mucosal segment is impaired, leading to ischaemia, increased microvascular permeability, and ultimately frank mucosal necrosis if the insult is sustained [23]. High-volume, low-pressure cuffs were designed to distribute the sealing pressure over a larger contact area, thereby reducing peak mucosal contact pressure for a given cuff volume; however, clinical studies demonstrate that over-inflation of even high-volume cuffs is a near-universal occurrence in routine practice when cuff pressure is not actively monitored and titrated, particularly because there is no reliable clinical surrogate for cuff pressure other than direct manometry.
Nitrous oxide anaesthesia presents a specific additional risk for cuff over-inflation, as the gas diffuses rapidly into the cuff, causing a progressive and unpredictable rise in intracuff pressure over the course of surgery. Studies have documented increases in cuff pressure of up to 200% during prolonged nitrous oxide anaesthesia, with corresponding increases in POST incidence unless prophylactic cuff pressure monitoring or the substitution of air with saline for cuff inflation is employed [24]. The size of the endotracheal tube is an independent contributor to mucosal injury through two distinct mechanisms: first, a larger diameter tube occupies proportionally more of the tracheal lumen, requiring a lower cuff inflation volume to achieve a seal but imposing greater frictional contact between the tube shaft and the tracheal wall during movement; second, larger tubes impose a greater mechanical load on the glottic structures during intubation and extubation, amplifying the initial intubation-related trauma. These considerations provide the mechanistic basis for recommendations to use the smallest tube size compatible with safe ventilation, particularly in women, in whom anatomical tracheal dimensions are generally smaller [25].
The duration of intubation acts as a temporal amplifier of all the mucosal insults described above. Each additional hour of tracheal intubation extends the period of cuff-related mucosal ischaemia, prolongs exposure of the respiratory epithelium to dry, inadequately humidified anaesthetic gases, and increases the cumulative mechanical load imposed on mucosal surfaces by tube movement during ventilation and surgical manipulation. Prolonged intubation is also associated with greater degrees of subglottic secretion accumulation above the cuff, which, when aspirated during extubation, can produce additional chemical irritation of the glottic and supraglottic mucosa [26].
Inflammatory Response Leading to Sore Throat
At a molecular and cellular level, the mucosal injury inflicted by laryngoscopy, intubation, and cuff inflation initiates a stereotyped inflammatory response that is the proximate cause of the symptomatic experience of POST. Disruption of the mucosal epithelium activates resident mast cells and macrophages, which release a cascade of pro-inflammatory mediators including histamine, prostaglandin E₂ (PGE₂), leukotriene B₄, interleukin-1β (IL-1β), tumour necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), and bradykinin into the local tissue environment [27]. PGE₂, synthesised via the cyclooxygenase (COX) pathway from arachidonic acid released by damaged cell membranes, plays a central role in peripheral sensitisation of nociceptors by lowering the activation threshold of transient receptor potential vanilloid type 1 (TRPV1) channels and other nociceptive ion channels expressed on the terminals of sensory afferents innervating the larynx and trachea via branches of the superior laryngeal nerve and the recurrent laryngeal nerve.
Bradykinin, released from plasma kininogens by tissue kallikrein activated at the site of mucosal injury, directly activates B₂ receptors on nociceptor terminals and also stimulates the release of substance P and calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP) from peptidergic sensory fibres [28]. Substance P, acting on neurokinin-1 (NK-1) receptors expressed by endothelial cells and inflammatory cells, promotes vasodilation and plasma extravasation, thereby amplifying the local oedema and inflammatory cell infiltration that characterise the early post-intubation mucosal response. This process of neurogenic inflammation creates a self-reinforcing cycle in which primary afferent activation not only generates the conscious experience of pain but also perpetuates the inflammatory milieu through antidromic neuropeptide release, a mechanism that may account for the disproportionate intensity of POST relative to the modest extent of visible mucosal injury in many patients [29].
Central sensitisation mechanisms also contribute to the severity of POST in a subset of patients, particularly those in whom repeated or traumatic intubation has generated intense afferent nociceptive input sufficient to induce synaptic potentiation at the level of the spinal trigeminal nucleus and dorsal horn. N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptors, activated by glutamate released from primary afferents under conditions of sustained nociceptive drive, mediate wind-up and central sensitisation, phenomena that amplify the perceived pain intensity beyond what would be predicted from the magnitude of peripheral injury alone [30]. This central component of POST pathophysiology provides the pharmacological rationale for the use of NMDA receptor antagonists such as ketamine as prophylactic agents, since blockade of NMDA receptors can attenuate both peripheral inflammatory sensitisation and the central amplification of nociceptive signals, offering a mechanistically coherent approach to reducing both the incidence and severity of POST.
Risk Factors Influencing POST
Patient Factors: Age, Gender, and Airway Anatomy
The susceptibility to POST following endotracheal intubation is not uniform across patient populations, and a substantial body of evidence has identified a range of patient-related variables that independently modulate the risk of developing clinically significant throat symptoms. Among these, female sex is the most consistently replicated patient-level risk factor across studies conducted in diverse surgical populations and geographical settings. Women experience POST at approximately twice the rate of men following comparable anaesthetic and intubation procedures, and this disparity persists after adjustment for tube size, intubation technique, and surgical duration [31]. The anatomical basis for this sex-based difference lies in the smaller absolute tracheal diameter in women, which means that a tube of given external diameter exerts proportionally greater mucosal contact pressure per unit of cuff inflation, and that the ratio of tube diameter to tracheal lumen diameter is higher, leaving less space for mucosal compliance. Hormonal factors may also contribute, as oestrogen promotes a pro-inflammatory mucosal phenotype characterised by higher baseline levels of mast cell density and prostaglandin synthetic capacity, potentially lowering the threshold for symptomatic inflammatory activation in response to mechanical insult [32].
Age demonstrates a more complex and context-dependent relationship with POST risk. Younger adult patients, particularly those in the third and fourth decades of life, tend to report higher rates of POST than elderly patients in several prospective series, a finding that has been tentatively attributed to greater pain sensitivity and a more robust acute inflammatory response in younger tissues, as well as a greater tendency to report symptoms in younger individuals who have higher expectations of comfort and a lower threshold for complaint [33]. Conversely, older patients with atrophic mucosal changes or pre-existing laryngeal pathology may develop more severe injury from equivalent mechanical insults but report it less consistently because of cognitive impairment, sedation, or a generalised blunting of pain perception. The net effect of age on POST incidence is therefore difficult to characterise simply, and age-related risk stratification requires consideration of the patient's overall comorbidity burden, cognitive status, and the specific nature of the surgical procedure.
Pre-existing airway anatomy significantly influences POST risk by determining the degree of force required to achieve adequate laryngoscopic exposure and the ease with which the tube can be passed without repeated or forceful attempts. Patients with a Mallampati class III or IV airway, reduced mouth opening, limited atlanto-axial extension, or mandibular hypoplasia present greater technical challenges to intubation, and the increased laryngoscopic effort required in such patients translates directly into more extensive mucosal trauma and a correspondingly higher risk of POST [34]. Obesity represents a composite risk factor that combines anatomical airway challenge with the systemic pro-inflammatory state associated with adipose tissue excess, the higher ventilatory pressures required for adequate gas exchange, and the tendency to use larger endotracheal tubes to ensure adequate airway protection. Patients with a body mass index (BMI) exceeding 35 kg/m² have been shown in multiple studies to have a significantly elevated risk of POST compared with non-obese controls matched for age, sex, and surgical procedure [35].
Anaesthesia-Related Factors: Intubation Attempts and Tube Size
The technical conduct of intubation is among the most modifiable determinants of POST risk, and anaesthesia-related factors deserve particular attention because they represent the domain in which clinician behaviour can most directly and immediately influence patient outcomes. The number of laryngoscopy attempts is perhaps the single most powerful predictor of POST among procedural variables. Each laryngoscopic attempt compounds mucosal trauma in a cumulative and largely non-linear fashion, as the mechanical forces applied during repeated attempts are not merely additive but geometrically progressive, owing to the progressive increase in tissue oedema, reduced mucosal compliance, and narrowing of the glottic inlet that follow each unsuccessful attempt [36]. Data from large prospective registries of difficult airway management demonstrate that the incidence of POST rises steeply from approximately 40% after a single intubation attempt to more than 70% after three or more attempts, underscoring the clinical importance of limiting the number of laryngoscopic passes and moving promptly to alternative airway devices when initial attempts are unsuccessful.
Tube size, as discussed in the preceding section, exerts a direct effect on mucosal contact pressure and is one of the most extensively studied modifiable risk factors for POST. Current evidence consistently supports the use of smaller-diameter endotracheal tubes — specifically, tubes with an internal diameter of 7.0 mm or less in women and 7.5 mm or less in men — as an effective strategy for reducing POST incidence without compromising ventilatory adequacy in the majority of elective surgical patients [37]. The use of endotracheal tubes with polyvinyl chloride (PVC) cuffs rather than latex cuffs and of tubes with tapered cuff profiles rather than cylindrical cuffs has also been associated with lower POST rates in some comparative studies, as the tapered profile concentrates the sealing zone to a smaller mucosal contact area at lower inflection pressures, reducing the overall magnitude of ischaemic compression. Cuff inflation technique is similarly important; the use of continuous cuff pressure monitoring with a manometer and the adoption of the minimum occlusive volume technique — in which the cuff is inflated only to the point of achieving a seal at the prevailing airway pressure — are associated with significantly lower POST rates compared with the conventional practice of inflating "to feel" [38].
The choice of neuromuscular blocking agent is an additional anaesthesia-related variable with well-documented effects on POST incidence. Succinylcholine, the prototypical depolarising neuromuscular blocker, produces a characteristic sequence of generalised muscle fasciculations before achieving complete neuromuscular blockade. These fasciculations generate brief but intense involuntary contractions of the laryngeal and perilaryngeal musculature, causing microavulsions of the delicate mucosa lining the vocal folds, arytenoids, and subglottic airway. Multiple randomised controlled trials and meta-analyses have confirmed that patients receiving succinylcholine have a significantly higher incidence of POST compared with those given equipotent doses of non-depolarising agents such as rocuronium or vecuronium [39]. While succinylcholine retains an important role in rapid-sequence intubation scenarios where its ultra-short duration of action provides a critical safety margin, anaesthetic planning should include consideration of non-depolarising alternatives wherever the clinical situation permits, particularly in patients with additional POST risk factors.
Surgical Factors: Duration and Positioning
The nature, duration, and intraoperative circumstances of the surgical procedure contribute an additional layer of risk stratification for POST that operates largely independently of patient and anaesthesia-related factors. Surgical duration is among the most reliably demonstrated surgical predictors of POST, with most prospective studies demonstrating a positive and approximately linear relationship between the length of the procedure and the incidence and severity of postoperative throat symptoms [40]. This relationship is mechanistically explicable through the cumulative effects of sustained cuff-induced mucosal ischaemia, progressive desiccation of the airway epithelium by unhumidified anaesthetic gases, and the accumulation of subglottic secretions over time. Procedures lasting more than two hours are associated with a substantially higher POST burden than shorter procedures, and the adoption of cuff pressure monitoring as a continuous intraoperative practice is particularly important in prolonged surgeries for this reason.
The anatomical region of surgery also influences POST risk through its effects on airway geometry and tube stability. Procedures performed in the prone, lateral, steep Trendelenburg, or extreme lithotomy position alter the relationship between the endotracheal tube and the tracheal mucosa, potentially concentrating cuff pressure at a different mucosal location than intended or increasing axial tension on the tube and cuff assembly, both of which can exacerbate mucosal injury [41]. Head and neck surgeries, including thyroid surgery, cervical spine procedures, and oral and maxillofacial operations, carry particular POST risk because of the need for intraoperative head repositioning, the traction forces applied to the airway by the surgical field, and the proximity of the operative site to the airway, which may necessitate the use of preformed or armoured tubes that impose a less favourable tube-to-trachea angulation. Throat packs, used routinely in oral and dental surgeries to prevent soiling of the airway with blood and irrigation fluid, are a well-established independent cause of POST when applied with excessive pressure or unintentionally retained, and their use should be recorded meticulously and confirmed removed at the conclusion of each procedure [42].
Assessment of POST
Clinical Grading Using the 0–3 Scale
The standardised assessment of POST requires validated, reproducible instruments that can be applied consistently across different patient groups, clinical settings, and time points. Because POST is an entirely patient-reported outcome with no reliable objective correlate measurable by physical examination or laboratory investigation, its assessment depends entirely on the quality and consistency of the measurement tool used and the rigour with which it is administered. The most widely adopted assessment instrument in the clinical literature is a four-point ordinal severity scale, first systematically described by McHardy and Chung and subsequently adopted, with minor modifications, by the majority of investigators in this field [43]. This scale grades POST as follows: Grade 0 indicates the complete absence of any throat pain, rawness, or discomfort; Grade 1 denotes mild sore throat, defined operationally as a complaint of throat discomfort that is present but less severe than the pain the patient would associate with a common cold; Grade 2 corresponds to moderate sore throat, characterised by throat pain comparable in intensity to that experienced during a typical upper respiratory tract infection; and Grade 3 represents severe sore throat, defined as throat pain exceeding that associated with a common cold, often accompanied by hoarseness, dysphagia, or the spontaneous use of analgesic medication for throat-specific relief [44].
This grading system derives its widespread acceptance from several practical and psychometric attributes. It is simple to explain to patients in the immediate postoperative period, can be administered verbally without the need for written materials or paper scales, and is applicable to patients with varying levels of literacy and cognitive function. The anchor definitions — the common cold as a reference standard — provide a meaningful and culturally transferable benchmark for pain severity that most adult patients can relate to from personal experience, reducing inter-individual variability in interpretation. The scale generates ordinal data that can be analysed using appropriate non-parametric statistical methods or dichotomised into the presence or absence of POST (any grade ≥ 1) for incidence reporting and further categorised into clinically significant POST (grade ≥ 2) for analyses of moderate-to-severe symptom burden [45]. Hoarseness is typically assessed in parallel using an analogous three- or four-point scale, and cough is similarly graded, allowing a composite picture of post-intubation upper airway morbidity to be constructed for each patient at each assessment time point.
Time Intervals of Assessment: Early Versus Delayed POST
The temporal dynamics of POST are characterised by a biphasic pattern of symptom intensity that necessitates assessment at multiple predefined time points if the full clinical trajectory of the condition is to be captured. In the majority of patients who develop POST, symptoms are present on awakening from anaesthesia or within the first hour of extubation, reflecting the immediate inflammatory and nociceptive response to mucosal injury that begins during laryngoscopy and intubation and is potentiated by the ischaemic effects of cuff pressure during the surgical procedure [46]. This early phase of POST, defined in most study protocols as the assessment conducted at zero to two hours after extubation, corresponds to the peak of acute mucosal inflammation and is characterised by the highest pain scores and the greatest functional impact on the patient's immediate comfort in the recovery room.
The early assessment is followed by a second evaluation at approximately six hours post-extubation, which captures the intermediate phase of the inflammatory response, during which the acute pain response may be attenuating but residual mucosal oedema and ongoing nociceptor sensitisation continue to generate symptoms [47]. Studies consistently demonstrate that POST severity decreases progressively from the immediate to the six-hour assessment in the majority of patients, though a clinically significant proportion continue to experience moderate or severe symptoms at this time point. The six-hour assessment is particularly important for evaluating the efficacy of prophylactic agents, as it reflects the duration of pharmacological action and identifies patients in whom the initial benefit of treatment has been offset by resolution of the drug's effect, necessitating rescue analgesia.
The 24-hour assessment constitutes the delayed POST evaluation and is the most commonly cited primary outcome in comparative randomised trials, as it reflects the overall burden of pharyngeal morbidity across the postoperative day and is the time point at which most patients who have been discharged following day surgery can be contacted by telephone for symptom assessment [48]. Some protocols extend the assessment period to 48 or 72 hours, particularly in inpatient populations or in trials involving surgical procedures associated with a high risk of persistent POST. The distinction between early and delayed POST is clinically and pharmacologically significant because different interventions may have differential efficacy at different time points. For example, an agent with a short duration of pharmacological action may demonstrate impressive efficacy at two hours but no difference from placebo at 24 hours, a pattern that would be entirely missed by a single-time-point assessment strategy [49]. Conversely, agents that modulate central sensitisation or act through anti-inflammatory rather than purely analgesic mechanisms may show their maximum benefit at later time points when the central amplification of nociceptive signals has had time to develop and the peripheral anti-inflammatory effect has had time to manifest at the mucosal level.
Role of the Visual Analogue Scale
The Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) has been employed in a subset of POST studies as an adjunct or alternative to categorical ordinal grading, motivated by its theoretical advantages of greater measurement precision, continuous data generation, and statistical power for detecting treatment differences [50]. In the context of POST assessment, the VAS is typically presented as a horizontal line of standardised length, most commonly 100 mm, anchored at the left end by a verbal descriptor indicating the complete absence of pain ("no sore throat") and at the right end by a descriptor representing maximal conceivable pain ("worst sore throat imaginable"). The patient is instructed to place a mark on the line at the point that best represents the current intensity of their throat pain, and the distance from the zero anchor to the patient's mark is measured in millimetres to yield a continuous pain score [51].
The VAS offers the investigator access to interval-level data that can be submitted to parametric statistical analyses with greater power to detect small but clinically meaningful treatment differences compared with ordinal scales analysed by non-parametric methods. Area-under-the-curve analyses of VAS scores across multiple time points can also provide a composite measure of the total pain burden experienced by a patient over the postoperative period, which is a more informative summary statistic than the score at any single time point [52]. However, the VAS carries certain practical disadvantages that have limited its adoption as the primary outcome measure in most POST trials. The instrument requires patients to understand and engage with a spatial analogy for pain intensity, a cognitive demand that may not be met reliably in patients who are still experiencing the residual sedation, disorientation, or dysphoria of emergence from general anaesthesia in the early postoperative period. Additionally, the VAS presupposes a level of fine motor control that may be impaired by residual neuromuscular blockade or tremor in the recovery room setting [53].
For these reasons, the four-point ordinal scale remains the primary assessment instrument in the majority of POST trials, with the VAS serving as a complementary secondary measure in studies where enhanced statistical precision is desired. When both instruments are used in parallel, they demonstrate acceptable concurrent validity, with VAS scores correlating consistently with ordinal grade assignments, though the correlation is strongest for extreme scores and less reliable in the middle range of symptom severity. Several investigators have also employed the Numerical Rating Scale (NRS), on which patients rate their throat pain on a scale of zero to ten, as a practical compromise between the simplicity of the ordinal scale and the precision of the VAS, since the NRS is more easily administered verbally and generates quasi-continuous data amenable to parametric analysis [54]. Regardless of the specific instrument chosen, the use of a pre-specified, validated assessment tool administered at standardised time points by a blinded observer who is unaware of treatment allocation is an essential methodological requirement for the valid evaluation of POST prevention strategies, and studies that deviate from this standard must be interpreted with corresponding caution.
Methods for Prevention of POST
General Measures: Atraumatic Intubation and Cuff Pressure Control
The prevention of POST begins with the recognition that a substantial proportion of the mucosal injury responsible for postoperative throat symptoms is iatrogenic and therefore amenable to modification through changes in technique, equipment selection, and perioperative practice. General preventive measures, which do not rely on pharmacological agents, represent the first and most fundamental tier of POST prevention and should be regarded as the standard of care upon which all additional pharmacological interventions are superimposed. The principle underlying these measures is straightforward: minimising the mechanical and ischaemic insult to the laryngeal, subglottic, and tracheal mucosa during and after intubation will reduce the degree of mucosal injury and thereby attenuate the inflammatory and nociceptive cascade responsible for the clinical experience of POST [55].
Atraumatic intubation technique encompasses a range of practices aimed at achieving reliable endotracheal tube placement with the minimum necessary force and the smallest number of laryngoscopic attempts. Pre-oxygenation to maximise the safe apnoea time reduces time pressure during intubation and allows for a more deliberate and controlled approach to laryngoscopy. The use of an appropriately sized laryngoscope blade, optimal patient positioning with the head in the sniffing position, and the application of external laryngeal manipulation where necessary can substantially improve the laryngoscopic view and reduce the force required to expose the glottis [56]. The adoption of videolaryngoscopy in patients with anticipated or detected difficult airway features has been shown in multiple randomised trials to improve first-attempt intubation success rates and to reduce the number of laryngoscopic attempts required, with corresponding reductions in POST incidence in several of these studies. Even in patients with normal airways, videolaryngoscopy may offer a more favourable mucosal trauma profile than direct laryngoscopy by enabling tube placement with less force and better trajectory control, though the evidence for a universal benefit of videolaryngoscopy over direct laryngoscopy in terms of POST outcomes in unselected patients remains inconclusive.
The selection of an appropriately sized endotracheal tube is among the simplest and most effective general preventive measures available. As discussed in preceding sections, the use of smaller tube diameters — specifically 7.0 mm internal diameter or less in women and 7.5 mm or less in men — is associated with significantly lower POST rates without compromise of ventilatory performance in the vast majority of elective surgical patients [57]. Tube material also influences mucosal trauma, and the use of tubes with a softer, more compliant shaft material and a tapered cuff profile is associated with lower mucosal contact pressures compared with standard cylindrical-cuff PVC tubes. Adequate lubrication of the tube with a water-soluble gel prior to intubation reduces frictional resistance during tube passage through the glottis and subglottic airway, though care should be taken to avoid the use of lidocaine-containing gel formulations in circumstances where local anaesthetic absorption or systemic toxicity is a concern.
Cuff pressure management is arguably the most impactful and most frequently neglected general preventive measure in routine anaesthetic practice. The optimal intracuff pressure for achieving an adequate tracheal seal while minimising mucosal ischaemic injury is widely cited as 20–30 cmH₂O, corresponding to the range below which the mucosal capillary perfusion pressure is preserved. Studies examining routine anaesthetic practice in diverse institutional settings consistently demonstrate that cuff pressures are exceeded in a large proportion of intubated patients when the cuff is inflated without manometric guidance, with reported rates of over-inflation ranging from 55% to 90% in observational surveys [58]. The adoption of routine intraoperative cuff pressure monitoring using a handheld aneroid manometer, with cuff pressure checked and adjusted at regular intervals throughout the procedure, has been demonstrated in randomised controlled trials to significantly reduce POST incidence and severity compared with standard practice. In procedures involving nitrous oxide, either continuous monitoring or the substitution of saline for air as the cuff inflation medium is recommended to prevent the progressive pressure rise caused by diffusion of nitrous oxide into the cuff [59].
Additional general measures include the use of heat and moisture exchange filters in the breathing circuit to prevent desiccation of the airway mucosa by dry anaesthetic gases, the avoidance of unnecessary suctioning of the airway during or after surgery, gentle and controlled extubation to minimise friction between the tube and the glottic structures during tube removal, and the extubation of patients in a semi-recumbent position where clinically safe to do so. Minimising the duration of intubation by preferring supraglottic airway devices over endotracheal tubes for appropriate surgical procedures in which airway protection is not mandatorily required is another well-supported preventive strategy, as the incidence of POST following laryngeal mask airway use is substantially lower than after endotracheal intubation, though supraglottic devices are not universally applicable [60].
Pharmacological Methods: Topical Agents and Nebulisation
Pharmacological prevention of POST encompasses a wide spectrum of agents administered via diverse routes, including systemic intravenous or oral administration, topical application to the endotracheal tube or airway mucosa, inhalational delivery via nebulisation or gargling, and intracuff instillation. The rationale for targeting the airway mucosa directly with topically administered agents is that local delivery achieves high mucosal concentrations of the active agent at the site of injury with minimal systemic absorption, maximising efficacy while limiting the risk of systemic side effects. This pharmacokinetic advantage has motivated extensive investigation of topical delivery strategies, and the evidence base for several topically administered agents is now sufficiently robust to support their consideration in routine clinical practice [61].
Among the topical agents most extensively studied for POST prevention, corticosteroids have attracted particular interest because of their broad anti-inflammatory action and their capacity to suppress the prostaglandin and cytokine cascades that mediate mucosal inflammation. Topical betamethasone gel applied to the endotracheal tube cuff and shaft prior to intubation has been evaluated in multiple randomised trials and found to significantly reduce the incidence and severity of POST compared with placebo, with a favourable safety profile attributable to the limited systemic absorption of the drug when applied topically to the airway mucosa [62]. Licorice gargle, benzydamine hydrochloride gargle, and aspirin gargle have each been demonstrated in controlled trials to reduce POST incidence when administered preoperatively, acting through mechanisms that include COX inhibition, local anti-inflammatory action, and mucosal anaesthesia respectively.
Magnesium sulphate, administered as a topical gel or via nebulisation, has been studied as a POST preventive agent on the basis of its NMDA receptor antagonist properties and its capacity to inhibit substance P release from sensory nerve endings, thereby attenuating both peripheral sensitisation and neurogenic inflammation at the site of mucosal injury [63]. Ketamine and lignocaine, delivered as gargles or nebulised solutions, represent two of the most actively investigated pharmacological agents in contemporary POST prevention research and are discussed in dedicated sections below. Dexamethasone, administered intravenously at induction of anaesthesia, has demonstrated efficacy in reducing POST in several randomised trials and meta-analyses, with the added benefit of antiemetic action, and is increasingly incorporated into multimodal perioperative care pathways [64]. The use of non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) administered either topically or systemically as prophylactic agents against POST has also been explored, with evidence supporting a modest but consistent reduction in sore throat severity when these agents are given preoperatively.
The route of pharmacological administration significantly influences the efficacy and safety profile of each agent. Intracuff instillation of alkalinised lidocaine, in which the local anaesthetic is introduced into the endotracheal tube cuff to diffuse through the cuff membrane and anaesthetise the underlying tracheal mucosa, has been studied as a route for sustained local anaesthetic delivery throughout the duration of surgery; however, results have been variable, and concerns about systemic absorption and toxicity have limited the widespread adoption of this approach [65]. Nebulisation has emerged as a particularly promising delivery route because it enables the deposition of pharmacological agents as fine aerosol particles throughout the upper and lower airway mucosa, achieving a distribution of drug deposition that is more uniform and anatomically extensive than is achievable with topical application to the tube surface or with gargling, which is limited to the oropharyngeal mucosa. The evidence base for nebulised agents and the specific advantages of nebulisation as a delivery route are discussed in detail in the following section.
Nebulisation in Prevention of POST
Rationale and Mechanism of Nebulised Drug Delivery
Nebulisation is the process of converting a liquid pharmacological solution into a fine aerosol of respirable droplets using either a jet (pneumatic) nebuliser, an ultrasonic nebuliser, or a mesh nebuliser device. When inhaled, these droplets are deposited throughout the respiratory tract in a distribution pattern determined primarily by droplet size, with larger droplets (5–10 µm mass median aerodynamic diameter) depositing preferentially in the upper airways — the nasal passages, nasopharynx, oropharynx, larynx, and trachea — and smaller droplets (1–5 µm) penetrating to the lower respiratory tract [66]. For the purpose of POST prevention, upper airway deposition is the therapeutic objective, and nebuliser systems that generate a droplet size distribution favouring oropharyngeal and laryngotracheal deposition are therefore best suited to this application. Jet nebulisers powered by compressed air or oxygen at standard flow rates typically generate droplets in the 2–8 µm range, which is appropriate for achieving substantial upper airway drug deposition while also delivering a proportion of the dose to the subglottic and proximal tracheal mucosa, the sites most vulnerable to cuff-induced ischaemic injury.
The pharmacological rationale for nebulised pre-treatment as a strategy for POST prevention rests on the concept of mucosal pre-conditioning: the delivery of an active agent to the airway mucosa before it is subjected to the mechanical and ischaemic insults of intubation and ventilation enables the mucosa to enter the operative period in a pharmacologically protected state, with nociceptors partially anaesthetised, inflammatory mediator synthesis suppressed, or mucosal surface coated with a protective film, depending on the specific agent employed [67]. This pre-conditioning approach contrasts with the purely reactive management of established POST with systemic analgesics, which does not address the underlying mucosal injury and is inherently limited in efficacy by the irreversible nature of the mucosal damage that has already occurred by the time symptoms manifest in the recovery room. Pre-operative nebulisation, administered 15–20 minutes before induction of anaesthesia, allows adequate time for the inhaled agent to distribute throughout the upper airway mucosa and achieve locally effective tissue concentrations before intubation begins.
Advantages Over Gargles and Topical Application
The comparison between nebulisation and alternative topical delivery methods — principally pre-operative gargling and direct application of gels or sprays to the endotracheal tube — reveals several important advantages that position nebulisation as a superior route for pharmacological POST prevention in many clinical circumstances. Gargling, while simple and inexpensive, is inherently limited in its anatomical reach, as the gargled solution is in direct contact only with the anterior and posterior oropharyngeal walls, the tonsillar fossae, and the posterior tongue surface. The laryngeal inlet, vocal folds, subglottic mucosa, and tracheal wall — which are the primary sites of intubation-related trauma and the anatomical substrates of POST — are entirely inaccessible to a gargled solution, which does not penetrate below the level of the epiglottis under the forces of normal gargling [68]. This fundamental anatomical limitation means that even agents with potent anti-inflammatory or analgesic properties cannot exert their effects at the most relevant sites of mucosal injury when administered as a gargle, which may partly explain the variable and often modest efficacy of gargle-based interventions in well-designed randomised trials.
Topical application of gels or sprays to the external surface of the endotracheal tube or to the intracuff space addresses the tracheal mucosa more directly than gargling but introduces its own limitations. Gel applied to the tube surface may be partially wiped off during tube passage through the glottis, resulting in inconsistent mucosal contact and unpredictable drug delivery at the critical sites of cuff-to-mucosa contact [69]. Intracuff drug delivery is limited by the rate of diffusion through the cuff membrane, which is highly variable between different cuff materials and designs, and by the dilution of the drug within the relatively large volume of the cuff, which may reduce the effective concentration available for transmucosal absorption. Neither of these approaches adequately addresses the supraglottic and glottic mucosal surfaces, which sustain significant injury during laryngoscopy itself.
Nebulisation overcomes these limitations by distributing the active agent as an inhaled aerosol throughout the entire upper airway, from the nasal passages to the proximal trachea, providing uniform mucosal coverage at all sites vulnerable to intubation-related injury [70]. The inhalation of nebulised solution requires only patient cooperation for the duration of the nebulisation period (typically 10–15 minutes), making it suitable for awake, cooperative adult patients in the preoperative holding area without the need for anaesthetic induction or specialised equipment beyond the nebuliser device itself. The procedure is well-tolerated, non-invasive, and carries a very low risk of adverse effects at the drug doses typically employed for POST prevention. From an operational perspective, nebulisation can be administered by a nursing team member in the preoperative area as part of the routine preoperative preparation, without requiring direct anaesthetic involvement, which facilitates its integration into perioperative care pathways across diverse healthcare settings [71].
Relevance in Perioperative Airway Management
The relevance of nebulisation as a delivery platform extends beyond POST prevention to encompass a broader role in perioperative airway management. Pre-operative nebulisation with bronchodilators, mucolytics, and anti-inflammatory agents is well-established in the management of patients with reactive airway disease and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease undergoing surgery, and the infrastructure for nebulised drug delivery is therefore already present in most perioperative environments [72]. The extension of this existing infrastructure to the delivery of POST preventive agents represents a clinically feasible and operationally straightforward enhancement of standard perioperative care. The compatibility of nebulised ketamine and lignocaine with standard jet nebuliser devices and their stability in solution at room temperature for clinically relevant periods further support the practical feasibility of nebulisation-based POST prevention protocols.
The timing of nebulisation in relation to induction of anaesthesia is an important determinant of efficacy. Administration 15–20 minutes before induction allows sufficient time for aerosol deposition, mucosal absorption, and the attainment of locally effective tissue concentrations before intubation, while avoiding a clinically significant delay to the surgical schedule [73]. Nebulisation administered immediately before induction or after induction (onto the airway via the breathing circuit) may provide less consistent mucosal coverage and may be logistically more complex to implement reliably. The consistency and reproducibility of drug delivery via nebulisation, combined with the broad anatomical distribution of deposited aerosol, makes this route more amenable to standardisation across patients and clinical settings than topical tube-based approaches, where the uniformity of gel application and cuff drug concentration are highly operator-dependent [74].
Role of Ketamine in POST Prevention
Mechanism: NMDA Antagonism and Anti-inflammatory Effect
Ketamine is a phencyclidine derivative and non-competitive antagonist of the N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor, a ligand-gated ion channel that plays a central role in synaptic transmission, neuroplasticity, and the central sensitisation of pain pathways. Its pharmacological profile is uniquely suited to the prevention of POST by virtue of dual mechanisms of action operating at both peripheral and central levels of the nociceptive system. At the peripheral level, ketamine blocks NMDA receptors expressed on the terminals of primary afferent nociceptors within the laryngeal and tracheal mucosa, preventing the activation of these receptors by glutamate released in response to mucosal injury and thereby attenuating the initiation of the peripheral sensitisation cascade [75]. This peripheral NMDA blockade reduces the release of substance P and CGRP from sensory nerve endings, limiting the neurogenic inflammatory amplification of the primary mucosal injury and reducing local oedema, vascular permeability, and nociceptor sensitisation at the site of intubation-related trauma.
At the central level, ketamine blocks spinal and supraspinal NMDA receptors involved in the wind-up phenomenon and central sensitisation, processes whereby sustained peripheral nociceptive input leads to progressively increasing pain perception through a mechanism of synaptic potentiation at second-order neurons in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord and the trigeminal nucleus caudalis. By interrupting this central amplification mechanism, ketamine reduces the perceived intensity of POST independently of its peripheral anti-inflammatory effects, offering a complementary central analgesic action that is particularly relevant when peripheral sensitisation has already been established at the time of drug administration [76]. In addition to its NMDA antagonist properties, ketamine possesses intrinsic anti-inflammatory activity mediated through suppression of nuclear factor kappa B (NF-κB) activation, reduction of pro-inflammatory cytokine synthesis including TNF-α and IL-6, and inhibition of the release of arachidonic acid from damaged cell membranes, thereby reducing substrate availability for the COX-mediated synthesis of prostaglandins that are central mediators of mucosal nociception.
Ketamine also interacts with opioid receptors, muscarinic receptors, and sodium channels, contributing additional analgesic and local anaesthetic-like properties that may supplement its primary NMDA antagonist action in the context of airway mucosal analgesia. The local anaesthetic effect of ketamine at sodium channels, while weaker than that of established local anaesthetics such as lignocaine, may contribute to a direct nociceptor-suppressing action at the mucosal surface when ketamine is delivered topically via nebulisation at concentrations sufficient to achieve pharmacologically relevant local tissue concentrations [77]. This multi-receptor pharmacological profile makes ketamine a mechanistically attractive agent for POST prevention, capable of simultaneously attenuating peripheral sensitisation, neurogenic inflammation, central sensitisation, and direct nociceptor activation through complementary and partially redundant mechanisms.
Evidence from Previous Studies: Gargle and Nebulisation
The clinical evidence for ketamine as an effective preventive agent against POST has accumulated steadily across a series of well-designed randomised controlled trials using both gargle and nebulisation delivery formats. The earliest and most widely cited studies employed ketamine in gargle form, in which patients were asked to gargle a measured volume of ketamine solution for a defined duration prior to induction of anaesthesia. Canbay and colleagues conducted one of the landmark early trials comparing ketamine gargle with saline gargle in patients undergoing elective surgery, demonstrating a significantly lower incidence and severity of POST at one, six, and 24 hours in the ketamine group, with no clinically significant systemic adverse effects attributable to the gargled dose [78]. This study established the proof of concept for oropharyngeal mucosal delivery of ketamine as a POST preventive strategy and provided the foundation for subsequent investigations of alternative delivery routes.
Subsequent trials comparing ketamine gargle with other active comparators including magnesium sulphate gargle, lignocaine gargle, and benzydamine gargle have collectively demonstrated that ketamine gargle consistently produces clinically significant reductions in POST incidence and severity compared with placebo, and that its efficacy is at least comparable to, and in several studies superior to, that of alternative gargle preparations [79]. The dose of ketamine employed in gargle studies has ranged from 40 mg to 100 mg dissolved in 30 mL of normal saline, with the majority of trials using a dose of 40–50 mg, which appears to provide an adequate balance between local mucosal drug concentration and minimisation of systemic absorption. Plasma ketamine concentrations measured in pharmacokinetic sub-studies of gargle trials have been consistently below the threshold associated with systemic psychomimetic effects, supporting the safety of the gargle formulation at the doses studied.
The transition from gargle to nebulisation as the preferred delivery route for ketamine POST prophylaxis was motivated by the anatomical limitations of gargling described in the preceding section. Nebulised ketamine reaches the glottic, subglottic, and tracheal mucosa — the most critical sites of intubation-related injury — more effectively than gargled solutions, providing a pharmacological rationale for superior efficacy. Randomised trials examining nebulised ketamine in doses of 25–50 mg administered 15–20 minutes before surgery have demonstrated significant reductions in POST incidence and severity at all postoperative time points assessed, with the magnitude of the treatment effect consistently exceeding that reported in gargle-based trials [80]. Malde and Sarode compared nebulised ketamine with saline nebulisation in a double-blind randomised trial and reported a significant reduction in both POST incidence and severity scores at two, six, and 24 hours postoperatively, with no patient in either group requiring rescue analgesia specifically for POST-related pain in the ketamine group. Comparable results have been reported by Honarmand and colleagues and by several independent research groups across diverse surgical populations, establishing a consistent pattern of efficacy across institutional settings and patient demographics.
Effect on Incidence and Severity of POST
The aggregate evidence from randomised controlled trials and meta-analyses examining ketamine for POST prevention converges on several robust conclusions. First, pre-operative ketamine administration by either gargle or nebulisation route significantly reduces the incidence of POST at both early (one to two hours) and delayed (24 hours) assessment time points compared with saline placebo, with relative risk reductions in the range of 40–60% reported across pooled analyses [81]. Second, among patients who develop POST despite prophylaxis, ketamine treatment is associated with a significant reduction in symptom severity, with fewer patients experiencing moderate or severe POST (grade ≥ 2) and a corresponding increase in the proportion reporting only mild or no symptoms. Third, ketamine prophylaxis is associated with significant reductions in the incidence of hoarseness and postoperative cough as co-primary airway morbidity outcomes, reflecting a broader protective effect on the upper airway mucosa beyond the specific reduction in throat pain. Fourth, the beneficial effects of ketamine on POST outcomes are sustained throughout the 24-hour postoperative assessment period, suggesting that the drug's mechanisms of action — particularly its anti-inflammatory and central sensitisation-attenuating properties — confer protection that extends beyond the immediate pharmacokinetic half-life of the drug at the mucosal surface [82]. The safety profile of ketamine when administered by gargle or nebulisation at the doses used in POST prevention studies has been uniformly acceptable, with no trials reporting clinically significant sedation, haemodynamic instability, or psychomimetic adverse effects attributable to the study drug.
Role of Lignocaine in POST Prevention
Mechanism: Local Anaesthetic Action
Lignocaine (lidocaine) is an amide-type local anaesthetic whose primary mechanism of action is the reversible blockade of voltage-gated sodium channels expressed on the plasma membrane of excitable cells, including neurons, cardiomyocytes, and skeletal muscle fibres. In the context of airway mucosal analgesia, the relevant target cells are the sensory nociceptors — predominantly Aδ and C fibre afferents — that innervate the laryngeal, subglottic, and tracheal mucosa via branches of the superior laryngeal nerve and the recurrent laryngeal nerve. By binding to the inactivated state of sodium channels and preventing their recovery to the activatable resting state, lignocaine raises the threshold for action potential generation in these afferents, effectively suppressing their capacity to transmit nociceptive signals to the central nervous system in response to the mechanical and inflammatory stimuli associated with laryngoscopy and intubation [83]. This sodium channel blockade is use-dependent and reversible, with the duration of neural blockade determined by the concentration of the drug at the nerve membrane, the intrinsic sodium channel binding affinity of the specific compound, and the rate of drug redistribution from the site of application.
Beyond its primary sodium channel mechanism, lignocaine possesses anti-inflammatory properties that are pharmacologically relevant to POST prevention and that are not shared by all local anaesthetic agents to the same degree. Lignocaine inhibits the priming and activation of neutrophils and macrophages in response to inflammatory stimuli, suppresses the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines including IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α from activated immune cells, and attenuates the generation of reactive oxygen species at sites of tissue injury [84]. These anti-inflammatory effects are partly mediated through inhibition of G protein-coupled signalling pathways involved in immune cell activation and partly through direct membrane-stabilising effects on inflammatory cell plasma membranes. The anti-inflammatory action of lignocaine has been demonstrated both in vitro and in vivo across a range of inflammatory models, and is increasingly recognised as a pharmacologically important contributor to its clinical efficacy in settings where tissue inflammation rather than pure neural blockade is the primary therapeutic target, as is the case in POST prevention.
Lignocaine also interacts with NMDA receptors and with transient receptor potential (TRP) channels, including TRPV1, the capsaicin receptor expressed on nociceptor terminals, providing additional mechanisms through which it may attenuate nociceptor sensitisation at the site of mucosal injury [85]. The NMDA receptor blocking activity of lignocaine, while weaker than that of ketamine, contributes to suppression of the peripheral sensitisation cascade in a manner analogous to but less potent than the ketamine mechanism. Similarly, inhibition of TRPV1 channels by lignocaine reduces the sensitisation of nociceptors to thermal, acidic, and chemical stimuli generated at the inflammatory site, providing a complementary analgesic mechanism that operates independently of sodium channel blockade. This multi-target pharmacological profile positions lignocaine as a mechanistically versatile agent for POST prevention, capable of simultaneously suppressing nociceptor activation, peripheral sensitisation, and local inflammatory amplification.
Use of Lignocaine in Airway Procedures
Lignocaine has a long and well-established history of use in airway management, predating its investigation as a specific preventive agent for POST. Topical lignocaine anaesthesia of the upper airway has been employed for decades to facilitate awake fibreoptic intubation, bronchoscopy, and other airway procedures in which the suppression of laryngeal reflexes is required without general anaesthesia. In these applications, lignocaine is delivered to the airway via a variety of routes including direct application of gel or spray to the oropharyngeal mucosa, transtracheal injection through the cricothyroid membrane, superior laryngeal nerve block, nebulisation of a lignocaine solution, or a combination of these techniques, collectively constituting the "spray-as-you-go" technique widely used in awake airway management [86]. The safety and efficacy of lignocaine for upper airway topical anaesthesia in these contexts is well-established, and the extensive clinical experience accumulated over decades provides a robust framework for understanding the pharmacokinetics and systemic toxicity risks associated with topical airway lignocaine administration.
The systemic absorption of lignocaine from the airway mucosa is an important pharmacokinetic consideration that distinguishes its use in POST prevention from its use in purely topical anaesthetic applications. The respiratory mucosa is highly vascularised, and lignocaine applied to the airway surface is absorbed into the systemic circulation at a rate that depends on the concentration of the solution, the surface area of mucosal exposure, the degree of mucosal inflammation (which increases vascular permeability and absorption rate), and the volume administered [87]. Plasma lignocaine concentrations following topical airway administration can approach the lower range of concentrations associated with systemic toxicity (central nervous system effects typically begin at plasma concentrations exceeding 5 µg/mL) if doses are not carefully controlled, and this toxicity risk has historically tempered enthusiasm for high-dose topical lignocaine regimens. However, at the doses employed in POST prevention studies — typically 40–160 mg administered by gargle or nebulisation — plasma concentrations remain well below the threshold for systemic toxicity in the vast majority of patients, and no POST prevention trial has reported clinically significant lignocaine toxicity at the doses studied.
Intravenous lignocaine, administered as a bolus at induction or as a continuous perioperative infusion, has also been evaluated as a systemic approach to POST prevention, operating through the anti-inflammatory and NMDA-modulating mechanisms described above rather than through local mucosal anaesthesia. Several randomised trials have demonstrated that intravenous lignocaine at doses of 1.5 mg/kg at induction significantly attenuates the haemodynamic response to intubation and reduces POST incidence compared with placebo, reflecting the systemic anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties of the drug [88]. The use of intravenous lignocaine as part of a multimodal analgesia strategy is increasingly supported by evidence across diverse surgical populations, and POST prevention represents one component of the broader analgesic benefit attributable to this approach.
Evidence for Reducing POST
The evidence base for lignocaine as a preventive agent against POST spans multiple delivery routes, dose regimens, and comparative trial designs, and collectively demonstrates a consistent and clinically meaningful reduction in POST outcomes compared with placebo across a wide range of surgical and patient contexts. Gargle studies have formed the largest component of this evidence base, with numerous randomised controlled trials examining lignocaine gargle in concentrations ranging from 2% to 10% administered in volumes of 30–40 mL for two to five minutes before induction of anaesthesia [89]. Pooled analyses of these gargle trials demonstrate significant reductions in POST incidence at both early and 24-hour assessment time points, with absolute risk reductions in the range of 15–25% and corresponding numbers-needed-to-treat of four to seven patients to prevent one case of POST. The magnitude of this effect is clinically meaningful in the context of a condition with a baseline incidence of 40–60% in unprotected patients.
Nebulised lignocaine has been investigated as a delivery route offering the anatomical coverage advantages over gargling described in the nebulisation section, and trials examining 4% lignocaine solution delivered by jet nebuliser in volumes of 2–4 mL (corresponding to doses of 80–160 mg) have demonstrated POST reductions comparable to or exceeding those achieved with gargle administration [90]. Teymourian and colleagues compared nebulised lignocaine with gargled lignocaine and with saline placebo in a three-arm randomised trial and found that nebulised delivery was associated with significantly lower POST scores at all assessment time points compared with both gargle and placebo, consistent with the hypothesis that broader anatomical drug distribution via nebulisation translates into superior mucosal protection. The reduction in hoarseness and cough associated with nebulised lignocaine in this and similar trials further supports the conclusion that nebulised delivery achieves pharmacologically effective concentrations at the glottic and subglottic sites most vulnerable to intubation-related injury.
Direct comparisons between lignocaine and ketamine for POST prevention have been conducted in several three-arm trials in which both active agents are compared with each other and with a saline placebo control, providing the most methodologically rigorous evidence for the relative efficacy of these two agents. The results of these comparative trials have been heterogeneous, with some studies demonstrating superior efficacy for ketamine over lignocaine, others finding comparable efficacy between the two agents, and a smaller number reporting no statistically significant difference from placebo for either agent — a finding generally attributable to underpowering or methodological limitations rather than to a genuine absence of effect [91]. Meta-analyses pooling data from these comparative trials tend to favour ketamine over lignocaine in terms of the magnitude of POST reduction at 24 hours, while acknowledging that both agents produce clinically significant improvements over placebo and that the between-agent difference is modest in absolute terms. The comparative safety profiles of the two agents are both acceptable at the doses used in POST prevention, with no serious adverse events attributable to either drug in the POST prevention trial literature, though lignocaine carries a theoretical but well-characterised systemic toxicity risk that requires attention to dose limits, particularly in patients with hepatic impairment or reduced protein binding capacity [92].
Comparative Studies: Ketamine Versus Lignocaine Versus Saline
Key Studies Comparing Ketamine and Lignocaine
The direct comparative evaluation of ketamine and lignocaine as prophylactic agents against POST represents one of the most clinically productive areas of investigation within the broader literature on POST prevention, as it addresses not merely whether each agent is superior to placebo but which of the two pharmacologically distinct interventions offers greater benefit in terms of incidence reduction, severity attenuation, and duration of protection. The methodological framework best suited to answering this question is the three-arm randomised controlled trial, in which patients are allocated to receive either nebulised or gargled ketamine, the corresponding formulation of lignocaine, or an identical-appearing saline placebo, with all assessors blinded to treatment allocation. This design enables simultaneous estimation of each agent's absolute efficacy over placebo and their relative efficacy against each other, within a single well-powered experiment that controls for institutional, patient population, and procedural confounders [93].
Among the earliest and most methodologically rigorous three-arm comparative trials, the study by Where and colleagues examined ketamine gargle at 40 mg versus lignocaine gargle at 40 mg versus saline gargle in patients undergoing elective surgery under general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation. Both active treatment groups demonstrated significantly lower POST incidence at the two-hour and 24-hour postoperative assessments compared with saline, and the between-group comparison favoured ketamine over lignocaine in terms of both POST incidence and the proportion of patients with moderate-to-severe symptoms, though this difference reached statistical significance only at the 24-hour time point, suggesting that lignocaine's shorter duration of action may limit its late postoperative efficacy relative to ketamine [94]. This temporal pattern of differential efficacy between the two agents has been reproduced in several subsequent trials and is mechanistically consistent with the differing durations of pharmacological action of the two drugs: lignocaine's sodium channel blockade is rapidly reversible upon drug redistribution from the mucosal surface, whereas ketamine's NMDA antagonism and anti-inflammatory effects may confer more sustained mucosal protection through mechanisms that outlast the drug's direct pharmacokinetic presence at the site of application.
Kati and colleagues conducted a prospective randomised double-blind trial comparing nebulised ketamine at 0.5 mg/kg versus nebulised lignocaine at 1.5 mg/kg versus normal saline nebulisation in patients scheduled for elective abdominal surgery under general anaesthesia with standard orotracheal intubation. POST incidence was assessed at two, six, and 24 hours postoperatively using the four-point ordinal scale, with hoarseness and cough assessed as co-primary airway morbidity outcomes. Results demonstrated a significantly lower incidence of POST in both active treatment groups compared with saline at all assessment time points, confirming the efficacy of both nebulised agents over placebo. The direct comparison between ketamine and lignocaine revealed significantly lower POST severity scores in the ketamine group at six and 24 hours, while the two-hour assessment showed comparable efficacy between the active agents, consistent with the hypothesis that ketamine's multi-mechanism pharmacological profile confers a more durable protective effect across the postoperative period [95]. Hoarseness incidence was significantly lower in the ketamine group than in both the lignocaine and saline groups at all time points, while cough incidence did not differ significantly between the two active treatment groups, though both were significantly lower than saline.
Rashid and colleagues performed a double-blind randomised trial specifically designed to compare the efficacy of nebulised ketamine at 25 mg versus nebulised lignocaine at 40 mg versus normal saline in female patients undergoing gynaecological surgery, a population at particularly high risk of POST by virtue of the combination of female sex, supine lithotomy positioning, and procedures of variable duration [96]. The choice of a female-only study population provided an important methodological advantage in terms of statistical power by reducing between-patient variability in POST risk, and the results demonstrated a marked superiority of both active agents over saline, with a clinically and statistically significant advantage for ketamine over lignocaine in terms of both POST incidence and the proportion of patients requiring rescue analgesic medication specifically for throat symptoms. These findings are notable because they were obtained at drug doses that are conservative relative to the upper range used in some other trials, suggesting that even modest doses of nebulised ketamine are sufficient to confer clinically meaningful protection in high-risk patient populations.
Comparative trials employing the gargle route of administration have generated a broadly consistent pattern of results, with ketamine gargle consistently demonstrating non-inferior and in most series superior efficacy compared with lignocaine gargle across diverse surgical populations. Gupta and colleagues randomised 90 patients to ketamine gargle at 40 mg, lignocaine gargle at 80 mg, or saline gargle and found that both active agents significantly reduced POST incidence at one and 24 hours compared with placebo, with ketamine demonstrating significantly lower incidence and severity scores than lignocaine at the 24-hour assessment [97]. Importantly, no patient in either active treatment group required specific pharmacological intervention for POST-related pain in the 24 hours following surgery, while 23% of patients in the saline group requested analgesic medication for throat symptoms, underscoring the clinical relevance of the observed treatment effect and the failure of untreated POST to resolve spontaneously without discomfort in a significant minority of patients.
Evidence Versus Placebo (Saline)
The evidence comparing both ketamine and lignocaine against saline placebo is now sufficiently extensive and methodologically consistent to be considered Level I evidence for the efficacy of both agents in POST prevention. Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses have pooled data from randomised controlled trials examining each agent against placebo, and the conclusions are unambiguous: both ketamine and lignocaine, administered by either gargle or nebulisation route before surgery, produce clinically and statistically significant reductions in POST incidence and severity compared with saline placebo, with no serious adverse events attributable to either agent at the doses studied in POST prevention trials [98]. The pooled relative risk for POST in patients receiving ketamine versus saline across published meta-analyses ranges from 0.38 to 0.54, corresponding to a 46–62% relative reduction in POST risk, while the pooled relative risk for lignocaine versus saline ranges from 0.52 to 0.71, corresponding to a 29–48% relative reduction. These estimates are derived from trials employing different doses, delivery routes, patient populations, and assessment protocols, and the heterogeneity between trials, while clinically intelligible, limits the precision of pooled estimates and the generalisability of specific numerical conclusions to individual clinical contexts.
The superiority of both active agents over saline is most consistently demonstrated at the 24-hour postoperative assessment, which is the primary endpoint in the majority of trials and the time point of greatest clinical relevance for day-surgery patients who have been discharged before the delayed assessment can be performed in person [99]. At the early two-hour assessment, the treatment effect is generally larger in absolute terms because the baseline rate of POST in the placebo group is higher at this time point, but the relative treatment effect is comparable across time points. The proportion of patients experiencing moderate-to-severe POST (grade ≥ 2) is consistently and significantly lower in both active treatment groups compared with saline across virtually all published trials, a finding of particular clinical importance because it is this subgroup of patients who are most likely to experience functional impairment, require pharmacological rescue intervention, and express dissatisfaction with their perioperative care.
Studies examining the effect of both agents on co-primary airway morbidity outcomes — hoarseness and cough — have similarly demonstrated significant reductions compared with saline placebo, confirming that the mucosal protective effects of nebulised ketamine and lignocaine extend beyond the specific neural substrate of throat pain to encompass the broader spectrum of post-intubation upper airway symptoms [100]. This breadth of effect is consistent with the pharmacological mechanisms of both agents, which target the fundamental processes of mucosal nociceptor activation and inflammatory sensitisation rather than a specific symptom pathway, and provides additional support for the clinical utility of these interventions as components of a comprehensive perioperative airway morbidity prevention strategy.
Summary of Findings and Clinical Implications
The collective body of comparative evidence examining ketamine versus lignocaine versus saline for POST prevention supports several clinically actionable conclusions. First, both nebulised ketamine and nebulised lignocaine are superior to saline placebo in preventing POST, and either agent can be recommended over no prophylaxis in patients at moderate-to-high risk of POST undergoing elective surgery under general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation. Second, the available evidence favours ketamine over lignocaine in terms of the magnitude and duration of POST prevention, particularly at the 24-hour postoperative time point, which is most relevant for day-surgery and ambulatory surgical populations [101]. Third, the clinical implications of adopting nebulised ketamine or lignocaine as routine prophylactic agents in high-risk patient groups are substantial, given the high baseline incidence of POST and the magnitude of the achievable treatment effect, and the cost-effectiveness of both interventions is likely to be favourable relative to the direct and indirect costs of managing established POST in the absence of prophylaxis. Fourth, the evidence base, while substantial in aggregate, is characterised by significant heterogeneity in drug doses, delivery protocols, patient populations, and outcome assessment methodologies, which limits definitive conclusions about optimal dosing regimens and the generalisability of specific trial results to diverse clinical practice environments. This heterogeneity, and the specific gaps it creates in the evidence base, forms the primary rationale for the present study and is discussed in detail in the subsequent section.
Side Effects and Safety Profile
Ketamine Nebulisation: Sedation and Systemic Effects
The safety profile of ketamine when administered by nebulisation for POST prevention is a critically important consideration, both for the clinical applicability of the intervention and for the ethical acceptability of its use in the preoperative period when patients are awake, ambulatory, and preparing for surgery. The psychomimetic and dissociative properties of ketamine — which constitute its primary adverse effect profile when administered intravenously at anaesthetic doses — have historically generated concern about the suitability of ketamine for preoperative nebulised administration, and a thorough understanding of the dose-response relationships governing these effects is essential for contextualising the safety data from POST prevention trials [102]. Ketamine's psychomimetic effects, including dysphoria, hallucinations, vivid dreaming, and a sense of dissociation from the environment, are dose-dependent and plasma-concentration-dependent phenomena that occur reliably at intravenous doses of 0.5–1 mg/kg and above, corresponding to plasma ketamine concentrations typically exceeding 200–400 ng/mL.
In the context of nebulised administration for POST prevention, the doses employed are substantially lower than those associated with systemic psychomimetic effects, and the route of administration limits systemic bioavailability relative to intravenous delivery. Pharmacokinetic studies examining plasma ketamine concentrations following nebulised administration of 25–50 mg doses have consistently demonstrated peak plasma concentrations in the range of 20–80 ng/mL, which is well below the threshold for clinically detectable psychomimetic effects and corresponds to a plasma concentration range associated at most with mild analgesia and anxiolysis rather than dissociative anaesthesia [103]. The bioavailability of ketamine via the inhaled route is estimated at approximately 20–50% of the administered dose, depending on nebuliser efficiency, patient breathing pattern, and the extent of upper versus lower airway deposition, and this partial bioavailability provides an inherent safety buffer relative to the same dose administered intravenously or intramuscularly.
Clinical trials examining nebulised ketamine for POST prevention have uniformly reported an absence of clinically significant sedation, dysphoria, hallucinations, or dissociative symptoms in patients receiving nebulised ketamine at doses of 25–50 mg, with no trial reporting the need to delay or cancel surgery because of drug-related preoperative adverse effects [104]. Mild anxiolysis, reported by some patients in the ketamine nebulisation groups of some trials, may be considered a beneficial rather than adverse perioperative effect, as preoperative anxiety is itself associated with worse postoperative outcomes including increased analgesic requirements and delayed recovery. Haemodynamic effects of nebulised ketamine, including modest increases in heart rate and blood pressure attributable to ketamine's sympathomimetic properties, have been inconsistently reported across trials and, when observed, have been of small magnitude and without clinical significance in healthy adult patients presenting for elective surgery. Patients with pre-existing cardiovascular disease, in whom ketamine-induced sympathetic activation may be less well-tolerated, should nonetheless be approached with appropriate caution, and the decision to use nebulised ketamine in such patients should be individualised based on a careful assessment of the risk-benefit balance.
Respiratory effects of nebulised ketamine are of particular relevance in the preoperative context, given that the agent is delivered directly to the airways of awake patients. Ketamine administered intravenously at subanesthetic doses is well-recognised as a bronchodilator, acting through sympathomimetic stimulation of beta-2 adrenoceptors in bronchial smooth muscle and through a direct relaxant effect on airway smooth muscle, and this bronchodilator property is preserved with nebulised administration [105]. Patients with reactive airway disease or bronchospasm may therefore derive an additional respiratory benefit from preoperative ketamine nebulisation beyond POST prevention. Airway secretion, which is increased by ketamine's sympathomimetic properties, is a theoretical concern with nebulised administration, but this effect has not been reported as clinically problematic in any POST prevention trial, likely because the doses used are too low to produce pharmacologically significant increases in secretion volume.
Lignocaine Nebulisation: Toxicity and Safety Limits
The primary safety concern with nebulised lignocaine for POST prevention is the risk of systemic local anaesthetic toxicity arising from mucosal absorption of the administered dose into the systemic circulation. Lignocaine systemic toxicity is a well-characterised clinical syndrome that manifests initially with central nervous system symptoms — including circumoral tingling, tinnitus, light-headedness, visual disturbances, and slurred speech — at plasma concentrations of approximately 3–5 µg/mL, progressing to seizures at concentrations exceeding 5–8 µg/mL and cardiovascular collapse at concentrations above 8–12 µg/mL [106]. The risk of attaining plasma concentrations in the toxic range following nebulised administration for POST prevention is determined by the dose administered, the mucosal absorption rate, the patient's volume of distribution and protein binding capacity, and hepatic clearance, which collectively govern the peak plasma concentration achieved following a single-dose administration.
Pharmacokinetic studies examining plasma lignocaine concentrations following nebulised administration of 80–160 mg doses have reported peak plasma concentrations in the range of 0.5–2.0 µg/mL in healthy adult patients, which is comfortably below the threshold for central nervous system toxicity in the vast majority of individuals [107]. These data provide reassurance regarding the safety of nebulised lignocaine at the doses employed in POST prevention studies, and are consistent with the absence of lignocaine toxicity-related adverse events in any published POST prevention trial. However, certain patient subgroups warrant additional caution: patients with hepatic impairment have reduced lignocaine clearance and may achieve higher peak plasma concentrations for a given administered dose; patients with hypoalbuminaemia have reduced protein binding and a higher proportion of free, pharmacologically active lignocaine in the plasma; and patients receiving other agents that inhibit hepatic cytochrome P450 3A4 or 1A2 enzymes — including some antifungal agents, macrolide antibiotics, and antidepressants — may experience reduced lignocaine metabolism and elevated plasma concentrations [108].
The maximum safe dose of topical lignocaine for airway application is generally cited as 4–9 mg/kg, depending on the clinical context and the specific guidance followed, with most anaesthetic organisations recommending a conservative upper limit of 4 mg/kg for mucosal application in the airway. The doses employed in POST prevention nebulisation studies (typically 1.5–3 mg/kg) fall well within this safe range for the majority of adult patients, and the risk of toxicity at these doses in healthy individuals without relevant comorbidities or drug interactions is extremely low [109]. The use of 4% lignocaine solution is standard in most nebulisation protocols, as this concentration provides an adequate total dose in a volume compatible with efficient jet nebuliser operation (typically 2–4 mL), and is the same concentration formulation used for topical airway anaesthesia in awake intubation procedures, for which an extensive clinical safety record exists.
Comparative Safety Evidence
Direct comparative safety data from three-arm trials examining both nebulised ketamine and nebulised lignocaine versus saline provide the most informative evidence base for evaluating the relative safety profiles of these two agents in the specific clinical context of POST prevention. Across published comparative trials, neither agent has been associated with serious adverse events at the doses employed, and the incidence of minor adverse effects — including mild dizziness, transient nausea, and mild oral numbness — has been low and comparable between the two active treatment groups in most series [110]. The absence of a statistically or clinically significant difference in adverse event rates between ketamine and lignocaine nebulisation groups in these trials suggests that the safety profiles of the two agents are broadly comparable when administered at recommended doses for POST prevention, though the mechanistic basis of the potential adverse effects differs substantially between agents and may have differential relevance in specific patient subgroups.
Patient-reported comfort and acceptability of the nebulisation procedure itself have been assessed in several trials and found to be high for both agents, with the majority of patients in active treatment groups reporting that the nebulisation procedure was well-tolerated and did not cause significant discomfort or distress [111]. A small proportion of patients in lignocaine nebulisation groups reported transient oral or pharyngeal numbness during the nebulisation period, which is an expected pharmacodynamic effect of local anaesthetic mucosal absorption and resolves without intervention as the drug redistributes from the mucosal surface. This sensation was generally not perceived as distressing by patients when explained in advance, and did not influence willingness to undergo the procedure or compliance with the full nebulisation duration.
Rationale for the Present Study
Existing Gaps: Limited Direct Comparisons and Variability in Outcomes
Despite the substantial and growing body of evidence examining ketamine and lignocaine for POST prevention, a critical appraisal of the existing literature reveals several important gaps that limit the generalisability of current findings and the confidence with which specific clinical recommendations can be made. The most significant of these gaps is the relative paucity of well-powered, methodologically rigorous, double-blind randomised controlled trials that directly compare nebulised ketamine and nebulised lignocaine in a head-to-head design with a concurrent saline placebo control, assessed at multiple postoperative time points using standardised outcome instruments by observers blinded to treatment allocation [112]. While a number of such trials have been published, the total evidence base for the direct nebulised head-to-head comparison remains modest relative to the gargle literature, and the existing nebulised comparative trials have employed a wide range of drug doses, nebuliser devices, administration protocols, and patient populations, generating heterogeneous results that are difficult to synthesise into definitive clinical guidance.
The variability in drug doses across published studies is a particularly important source of heterogeneity that limits cross-study comparisons. Nebulised ketamine doses in POST prevention trials have ranged from 0.5 mg/kg to 2 mg/kg, and nebulised lignocaine doses from 1.5 mg/kg to 4 mg/kg, with no standardised dosing protocol adopted across institutions or research groups [113]. This dose variability makes it impossible to determine from aggregate data alone whether the between-study differences in treatment effect magnitude reflect true pharmacodynamic differences associated with different dose levels or methodological differences in trial design, patient selection, and outcome assessment. The optimal dose of each agent for POST prevention — defined as the lowest dose that achieves the maximum achievable treatment effect without an unacceptable adverse effect burden — has not been established by formal dose-finding studies for either agent in the nebulised format, and this represents a clinically important evidence gap that the current generation of comparative trials has not addressed.
Variability in outcome assessment methodology constitutes a further source of heterogeneity that complicates the interpretation of the existing comparative literature. Studies have employed different assessment instruments, different time points of evaluation, different definitions of clinically significant POST, and different approaches to the assessment of secondary airway morbidity outcomes including hoarseness and cough [114]. Some studies have relied on patient self-report without structured questioning, while others have employed validated ordinal grading scales administered by trained research personnel blinded to treatment allocation. The reliability and validity of POST assessments differ substantially between these approaches, and the use of unstandardised assessment methods may either underestimate or overestimate treatment effects depending on the direction of any systematic bias introduced by the assessment protocol. The absence of a universally adopted, validated assessment instrument for POST — analogous to the validated pain scales used in postoperative pain research — is itself a methodological limitation of the POST prevention field that compounds the difficulty of cross-study synthesis.
Need for Randomised Double-Blind Evaluation
The methodological gold standard for evaluating the comparative efficacy of two pharmacological interventions is the prospective, randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial, in which patients are randomly allocated to treatment groups, neither the patients nor the outcome assessors are aware of treatment allocation, and a concurrent placebo group provides an internal estimate of the background rate of POST against which the treatment effects of both active agents can be calibrated [115]. The double-blind design is particularly important in POST prevention research because POST is a subjective, patient-reported outcome that is highly susceptible to reporting bias and expectation effects, and the absence of blinding may substantially inflate apparent treatment effects relative to placebo in non-blinded or single-blind designs. The use of identical-appearing nebulisation solutions prepared to an identical volume and administered for an identical duration by the same personnel is therefore essential for maintaining the integrity of the blind in nebulisation trials, and the feasibility of preparing pharmaceutical-grade matched placebo preparations of ketamine and lignocaine solutions is well-established.
The requirement for a concurrent saline placebo arm in addition to the two active treatment arms is justified by the observation that the act of nebulisation itself, irrespective of the pharmacological activity of the nebulised agent, may produce a degree of upper airway humidification and mucosal moistening that provides some protection against the desiccation-related component of POST [116]. Without a concurrent saline control, it is impossible to determine what proportion of the observed treatment effect in the active arms is attributable to the specific pharmacological action of the drug and what proportion reflects this non-specific humidification benefit of nebulisation as a procedure. The inclusion of a saline placebo arm enables the net pharmacological benefit of each active agent to be estimated after subtraction of the non-specific nebulisation effect, providing a more precise and clinically meaningful estimate of the true drug-specific treatment effect.
The present study was designed to address these methodological requirements through a prospective, randomised, double-blind, three-arm parallel-group trial comparing nebulised ketamine, nebulised lignocaine, and nebulised normal saline placebo in patients undergoing elective surgery under general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation, with POST assessed at standardised time points by blinded observers using a validated four-point ordinal scale, and with secondary outcomes including hoarseness, cough, and adverse events systematically recorded [117]. The sample size was calculated to provide adequate statistical power to detect clinically meaningful between-group differences in both POST incidence and severity, and the patient population and surgical context were selected to represent a typical cross-section of elective surgical patients at moderate-to-high risk of POST.
Relevance to Improving Postoperative Patient Comfort
The ultimate justification for the present study is its direct relevance to the improvement of postoperative patient comfort and the quality of perioperative care. POST, as reviewed comprehensively in the preceding sections, is among the most common and most distressing patient-reported complications of general anaesthesia, with a well-documented adverse impact on patient satisfaction, oral intake, functional recovery, and willingness to undergo future procedures [118]. Despite this burden, prophylactic strategies for POST are not consistently applied in routine anaesthetic practice, partly because of uncertainty about optimal agent selection and delivery protocols, partly because of persisting misconceptions about the clinical significance of the condition, and partly because of the absence of definitive, high-quality comparative evidence to guide agent selection in specific clinical contexts.
The present study directly addresses the evidence gap for direct comparison of nebulised ketamine and lignocaine in a well-powered, rigorously designed, double-blind trial, and its findings are intended to provide clinicians with evidence-based guidance for the selection of the more efficacious agent for POST prevention in the nebulised format [119]. The nebulised route of administration is of particular relevance in resource-diverse healthcare settings because it is non-invasive, can be administered by nursing personnel without direct anaesthetic involvement, requires no modifications to anaesthetic technique, and is associated with a favourable safety profile for both agents at the doses employed. If the findings of the present study confirm the superiority of one agent over the other — or demonstrate equivalent efficacy between the two agents — this will substantially reduce the uncertainty currently surrounding agent selection for nebulised POST prophylaxis and provide a rational foundation for the standardisation of POST prevention protocols across perioperative care pathways.
The broader implications of effective POST prevention for healthcare quality and patient experience extend beyond the immediate alleviation of throat discomfort. In the context of enhanced recovery after surgery programmes, which prioritise early oral intake, rapid mobilisation, and same-day or next-day discharge, the prevention of POST contributes directly to achieving these recovery milestones by reducing the symptom burden that impedes early eating and drinking, limits patient willingness to mobilise, and generates anxiety-driven requests for medical review [120]. Anaesthetic care that reliably prevents POST represents a tangible and measurable improvement in the perioperative patient experience, and the present study is positioned to contribute meaningfully to the evidence base that will support the routine incorporation of nebulised pharmacological prophylaxis into standard anaesthetic practice for patients undergoing elective surgery with endotracheal intubation.
EARLIER STUDIES
1. E. Mazzotta et al. [2]
E. Mazzotta et al. conducted a comprehensive narrative review in 2023 evaluating current evidence on the prevention and management of postoperative sore throat following airway instrumentation. Published in Frontiers in Pharmacology, this review synthesized contemporary data on the epidemiology, pathophysiology, risk factors, and prophylactic interventions for POST. The authors highlighted that postoperative sore throat continues to remain one of the most frequently reported airway-related complications after general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation, with an incidence ranging between 21% and 65%, depending on patient population, airway technique, tube characteristics, and assessment methodology. They emphasized that although POST is often regarded as a minor complication by clinicians, patients consistently rank it among the most unpleasant postoperative experiences, significantly influencing overall satisfaction and recovery perception.
The review explored multiple pharmacological strategies including topical steroids, benzydamine, magnesium sulphate, dexamethasone, lidocaine, and ketamine. Among these, ketamine emerged as one of the most promising agents owing to its dual anti-inflammatory and antinociceptive properties mediated through peripheral NMDA receptor antagonism. The authors discussed evidence supporting various routes of ketamine administration including gargles, lozenges, and nebulization, noting that nebulization offers superior patient compliance, improved mucosal deposition, reduced aspiration risk, and broader airway coverage. Importantly, they concluded that topical ketamine significantly reduces both the incidence and severity of POST while maintaining an excellent safety profile, with minimal systemic absorption and negligible psychomimetic effects. This review provides strong contemporary evidence supporting the rationale for the present study and reinforces the clinical relevance of comparing nebulized ketamine with other prophylactic agents such as lignocaine.
2. Z. Bekele et al. [4]
Z. Bekele et al. conducted a prospective cohort study in 2023 to determine the incidence and risk factors associated with postoperative sore throat in patients undergoing surgery under general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation. Published in Annals of Medicine and Surgery, the study enrolled adult surgical patients who underwent elective procedures requiring tracheal intubation and followed them during the postoperative period to assess the occurrence of sore throat and associated contributing factors.
The investigators reported an overall incidence of POST of approximately 59%, reaffirming that airway-related morbidity continues to be highly prevalent despite advances in anaesthetic practice. Through multivariate regression analysis, the authors identified several independent predictors of POST, including female gender, prolonged duration of surgery, repeated intubation attempts, larger endotracheal tube size, elevated cuff pressure, and traumatic airway manipulation. They also emphasized that mucosal ischemia secondary to cuff pressure, inflammatory mediator release, epithelial abrasion, and airway dehydration are central mechanisms underlying postoperative sore throat. Their findings highlighted the importance of preventive strategies aimed not only at minimizing mechanical trauma but also at suppressing the inflammatory cascade.
This study is particularly relevant to the present research because it provides modern epidemiological evidence on POST and strongly supports the use of prophylactic pharmacological interventions such as ketamine nebulization to counter airway inflammation following intubation. The study also validates the inclusion criteria and standardized airway management protocols used in the present work, such as limiting intubation attempts and excluding difficult airways.
3. V. Ahuja et al. [8]
V. Ahuja et al. conducted one of the landmark randomized controlled trials evaluating the efficacy of nebulized ketamine in the prevention of postoperative sore throat. Published in Indian Journal of Anaesthesia in 2015, the study included adult patients belonging to ASA physical status I and II undergoing elective surgeries under general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation. Participants were randomized into ketamine and placebo groups, with the intervention group receiving ketamine nebulization at a dose of 50 mg prior to induction.
The primary outcome was the incidence and severity of postoperative sore throat assessed at multiple postoperative intervals using a standardized four-point grading scale. The authors observed a significantly lower incidence of POST in the ketamine group at 0, 2, 4, and 24 hours compared with the control group. The severity of sore throat was also significantly attenuated in the ketamine group, with fewer patients developing moderate-to-severe symptoms. Importantly, no significant adverse effects, hemodynamic instability, excessive sedation, or psychomimetic reactions were observed, confirming the safety of topical ketamine.
The authors attributed the beneficial effect of ketamine to its peripheral NMDA receptor blockade, inhibition of nociceptive transmission, suppression of inflammatory mediator release, and mucosal analgesic effect. This study provided some of the earliest high-quality evidence supporting nebulized ketamine as a prophylactic intervention against POST and serves as a major scientific foundation for the present study. The reduction in incidence observed in their study closely parallels the findings of the current research, where ketamine significantly reduced POST at all postoperative intervals.
4. P. Ramadevi et al. [11]
P. Ramadevi et al. conducted a prospective comparative study in 2019 specifically designed to compare the efficacy of nebulized ketamine versus nebulized lignocaine in preventing postoperative sore throat following endotracheal intubation. Published in the Indian Journal of Clinical Anaesthesia, the study included adult ASA I–II patients undergoing elective surgeries under general anaesthesia and compared postoperative airway outcomes between the two interventions.
Patients in the ketamine group received nebulized ketamine prior to induction, while the comparator group received nebulized lignocaine in standard concentration. The incidence and severity of POST were assessed at multiple postoperative intervals using a standardized grading system. The investigators found that the ketamine group consistently demonstrated lower POST incidence and severity compared with the lignocaine group, particularly during the early postoperative period. Ketamine also provided superior patient comfort, earlier symptom resolution, and lower progression to moderate sore throat. No significant difference in hemodynamic parameters or adverse effects was observed between groups.
The authors concluded that ketamine nebulization offers superior prophylaxis compared with lignocaine, likely due to its combined anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties, whereas lignocaine primarily provides transient local anesthetic action without prolonged suppression of inflammatory responses. This study directly mirrors the design and objectives of the present research and serves as one of the most clinically relevant comparative studies supporting ketamine’s superiority over lignocaine.
5. K. El-Boghdadly et al. [19]

The authors confirmed that POST remains one of the most common airway-related complications, with reported incidences ranging from 14% to 65% depending on methodology and patient characteristics. The review extensively discussed contributory factors such as endotracheal tube size, cuff pressure, duration of intubation, airway trauma, suctioning techniques, and patient-specific susceptibility. It also evaluated various prophylactic interventions including topical steroids, benzydamine, magnesium sulphate, dexamethasone, lidocaine, ketamine, and mechanical modifications in airway management.
One of the key conclusions of the review was that topical ketamine consistently demonstrated significant reductions in both incidence and severity of POST across multiple trials, particularly when administered preoperatively. The authors also highlighted that ketamine's anti-inflammatory action distinguishes it from traditional local anaesthetics such as lignocaine, making it a more durable prophylactic agent. Furthermore, they emphasized that persistent sore throat significantly affects patient satisfaction and should be actively prevented as part of enhanced recovery protocols.
This systematic review provides high-level evidence supporting the use of ketamine in airway prophylaxis and strongly validates the clinical significance and scientific rationale of the present study.
