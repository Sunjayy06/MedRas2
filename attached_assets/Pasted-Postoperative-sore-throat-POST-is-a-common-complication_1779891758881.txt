Postoperative sore throat (POST) is a common complication following general anesthesia with endotracheal intubation, characterized by discomfort, irritation, or pain in the throat. The incidence of POST has been reported to range from 21% to 65% globally, depending on patient factors, intubation technique, and anesthetic management [1]. In India, similar prevalence rates have been observed, underscoring the clinical significance of this condition [7]. Although often regarded as a minor complication, POST can adversely affect patient satisfaction and recovery, highlighting the need for effective preventive strategies. The impact of POST extends beyond physical discomfort, as it may delay recovery, prolong hospital stays, and increase healthcare costs. Patients experiencing POST often report reduced satisfaction with their perioperative care, which may influence their perception of surgical outcomes [2, 9]. Furthermore, severe cases of POST can lead to complications such as dysphagia, hoarseness, or even airway injury, thereby exacerbating the healthcare burden [8]. Addressing this issue is essential to improve postoperative quality of life and optimize functional recovery. The pathophysiology of POST involves multiple mechanisms, including mechanical trauma to the mucosa during intubation, inflammation, and nociceptive activation. Endotracheal intubation causes epithelial disruption and local inflammation, leading to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-6 and tumor necrosis factor-alpha [9]. Additionally, activation of N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptors has been implicated in the nociceptive pathways associated with POST [3]. These mechanisms provide a basis for targeted interventions aimed at reducing mucosal injury and inflammation. Several assessment tools and preventive strategies have been employed to address POST. Visual analog scales (VAS) are commonly used to quantify the severity of sore throat, while Ramsay sedation scores and hemodynamic parameters are monitored to evaluate patient comfort [1]. Pharmacological interventions such as nebulized ketamine, lignocaine, dexamethasone, and magnesium sulfate have demonstrated varying degrees of efficacy in reducing POST incidence [2, 4]. However, limitations such as inconsistent results, potential side effects, and variable patient acceptance necessitate further investigation into optimal preventive measures. Ketamine, an NMDA receptor antagonist, has been proposed as a promising agent for POST prevention due to its anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties. Nebulized ketamine may reduce mucosal inflammation and nociceptive activation, thereby mitigating the severity of POST [1, 5]. Lignocaine, a local anesthetic, acts by stabilizing neuronal membranes and reducing sensory nerve activation, offering another potential preventive strategy [10]. Saline nebulization, although often used as a control, may provide symptomatic relief through mucosal hydration [7]. The comparative efficacy of these agents warrants systematic evaluation. International evidence has highlighted the effectiveness of preoperative nebulization in reducing POST incidence. Prasant et al. [1] conducted a randomized study comparing ketamine and lignocaine nebulization, reporting a significant reduction in moderate-to-severe POST with ketamine (8.9% at 6 hours) compared to lignocaine (28.9%). Similarly, Sharma and Malav [4] demonstrated superior efficacy of ketamine over magnesium sulfate in reducing POST severity, with ketamine achieving a 40% reduction in incidence at 24 hours. Salhan and Ahuja [5] explored ketamine-clonidine combinations, finding ketamine alone to be more effective in reducing POST severity. These findings underscore the potential of ketamine as a prophylactic agent. Indian studies have provided additional insights into POST management. Vaidiyanathan et al. [6] compared lignocaine gargles with ketamine gargles, reporting better outcomes with ketamine in terms of reduced POST severity and patient satisfaction. Saradas and Padmavathi [7] evaluated ketamine and saline nebulization, confirming ketamine's superior efficacy in reducing POST incidence. Despite these contributions, gaps remain in the comparative evaluation of nebulized ketamine, lignocaine, and saline within the Indian context, particularly in tertiary care settings. The present study, conducted at Thanjavur Medical College Hospital, aims to evaluate the comparative efficacy of preoperative nebulization with ketamine, 4% lignocaine, and saline in preventing postoperative sore throat among patients undergoing elective surgeries under general anesthesia with endotracheal intubation.
