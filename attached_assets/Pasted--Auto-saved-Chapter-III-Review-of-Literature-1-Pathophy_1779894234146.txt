### Chapter III: Review of Literature #### **1. Pathophysiology and Clinical Significance of Postoperative Sore Throat (POST)** Postoperative sore throat (POST) has been recognized as a frequent and distressing complication following general anesthesia with endotracheal intubation. The incidence of POST has been reported to range between 21% and 65%, depending on various factors such as patient characteristics, type of surgery, and anesthetic techniques employed [1]. The condition arises primarily due to mechanical and chemical irritation of the tracheal mucosa caused by the endotracheal tube, leading to inflammation and nociceptive activation. This inflammatory response may be exacerbated by factors such as prolonged intubation, excessive cuff pressure, and repeated airway manipulations [9]. Several studies have highlighted the clinical importance of addressing POST, as it significantly impacts patient satisfaction and recovery. Although often considered a minor complication, the discomfort associated with POST can lead to increased postoperative morbidity and delayed discharge. The pathophysiology of POST involves a complex interplay of mechanical trauma, local ischemia, and the release of inflammatory mediators, which collectively contribute to mucosal injury and pain perception [3]. The need for effective prophylactic strategies to mitigate POST has been underscored by numerous investigations. Various pharmacological and non-pharmacological interventions have been explored, with mixed results. This underscores the necessity for further research to identify optimal preventive measures, particularly in the context of different patient populations and surgical settings. #### **2. Pharmacological Interventions for POST Prevention** Pharmacological approaches to prevent POST have primarily focused on agents with anti-inflammatory, analgesic, or local anesthetic properties. Among these, ketamine and lignocaine have garnered significant attention due to their distinct mechanisms of action and potential efficacy. Ketamine, an N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor antagonist, has been shown to possess both anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties. Studies have demonstrated that preoperative nebulization with ketamine can effectively reduce the incidence and severity of POST. For instance, Prasant et al. [1] conducted a randomized controlled trial comparing ketamine and 4% lignocaine nebulization. The results indicated that ketamine provided superior prophylaxis against moderate-to-severe POST, with significantly lower incidence rates at 6 and 24 hours postoperatively. This finding is consistent with the anti-nociceptive and anti-inflammatory effects of ketamine, which may attenuate the mucosal injury caused by intubation. Lignocaine, a widely used local anesthetic, has also been investigated for its efficacy in preventing POST. Lignocaine acts by stabilizing neuronal membranes and reducing nociceptive transmission. A systematic review by Liao et al. [10] highlighted the effectiveness of lignocaine lubricants in reducing intubation-related complications, including POST. However, the study noted variability in outcomes depending on the mode of administration and concentration of lignocaine used. Comparative studies have further elucidated the relative efficacy of these agents. For example, Sharma and Malav [4] compared ketamine and magnesium sulfate nebulization, finding that ketamine was more effective in reducing both the incidence and severity of POST. Similarly, Vaidiyanathan et al. [6] reported that ketamine gargle was superior to lignocaine viscous gargle in preventing POST following laryngeal mask airway insertion. These findings suggest that ketamine may offer distinct advantages over lignocaine in certain clinical scenarios. #### **3. Non-Pharmacological Strategies for POST Prevention** In addition to pharmacological interventions, non-pharmacological strategies have been explored to mitigate POST. These include optimizing intubation techniques, using smaller endotracheal tubes, and employing cuff pressure monitoring. While these measures can reduce mechanical trauma to the airway, their effectiveness in completely preventing POST remains limited. Recent studies have also investigated the role of alternative therapies, such as dexamethasone and magnesium sulfate nebulization. Kumari et al. [2] conducted a double-blind study comparing dexamethasone and magnesium sulfate nebulization. The results demonstrated that dexamethasone significantly reduced the incidence and severity of POST at multiple time points postoperatively. This effect was attributed to the potent anti-inflammatory properties of dexamethasone, which may counteract the inflammatory cascade triggered by intubation. Similarly, a meta-analysis by Makkar et al. [3] evaluated the efficacy of topical magnesium in preventing POST. The analysis revealed that magnesium, as an NMDA receptor antagonist, exhibited anti-nociceptive and anti-inflammatory effects, thereby reducing the incidence of POST. However, the study emphasized the need for further research to establish standardized dosing protocols and administration techniques. #### **4. Indian Studies on POST Prevention** Several Indian studies have contributed to the understanding of POST prevention, particularly in the context of ketamine and lignocaine nebulization. Prasant et al. [1] conducted a prospective study in an Indian population, demonstrating the superior efficacy of ketamine nebulization compared to lignocaine in preventing POST. The study highlighted the importance of tailoring interventions to the specific needs of the patient population, considering factors such as baseline risk and comorbidities. Asha and Padmavathi [7] investigated the incidence and severity of POST following nebulization with ketamine and saline. The results indicated that ketamine significantly reduced the incidence of POST compared to saline, reinforcing its potential as a prophylactic agent. These findings align with international studies and underscore the relevance of ketamine in the Indian clinical setting. #### **5.
