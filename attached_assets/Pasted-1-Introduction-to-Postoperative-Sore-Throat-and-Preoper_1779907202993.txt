1. Introduction to Postoperative Sore Throat and Preoperative Nebulization Postoperative sore throat (POST) is a frequent and often distressing complication following general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation. Despite being considered a minor adverse effect, POST can significantly impact patient satisfaction and recovery experience. The reported incidence of POST varies widely, ranging from 21% to 65%, depending on factors such as patient characteristics, anaesthetic techniques, and the type of airway management employed [1, 3]. The condition is characterised by discomfort or pain in the throat, often accompanied by hoarseness, cough, or dysphagia, and typically manifests within the first 24 hours post-extubation [3]. The pathophysiology of POST is multifactorial, involving mechanical trauma to the pharyngeal and laryngeal mucosa during intubation, as well as inflammation triggered by the endotracheal tube. Factors such as the size of the tube, cuff pressure, duration of intubation, and patient positioning during surgery contribute to the severity of POST [3]. Additionally, the use of dry gases and inadequate humidification during anaesthesia may exacerbate mucosal irritation [3]. Given its high prevalence and potential to impair postoperative recovery, effective strategies to prevent POST have garnered significant attention in clinical research. Preoperative nebulization has emerged as a promising intervention for the prevention of POST. Nebulization involves the delivery of pharmacological agents in aerosolised form, allowing direct application to the respiratory mucosa. This method offers several advantages, including ease of administration, rapid onset of action, and minimal systemic side effects [1, 6]. Various agents, including ketamine, lignocaine, magnesium sulphate, and dexamethasone, have been investigated for their efficacy in reducing the incidence and severity of POST [1, 2, 7]. Among these, ketamine and lignocaine have shown particular promise due to their unique pharmacological properties. Ketamine, an N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor antagonist, possesses both anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties, making it a suitable candidate for POST prevention. By modulating nociceptive pathways and reducing local inflammation, ketamine nebulization has been demonstrated to lower the incidence of moderate-to-severe POST compared to other agents [1, 4]. Lignocaine, a widely used local anaesthetic, acts by stabilising neuronal membranes and inhibiting sodium ion influx, thereby reducing pain and irritation. Studies have shown that lignocaine nebulization can effectively alleviate POST symptoms, although its efficacy may be inferior to that of ketamine in some contexts [1, 5]. The comparative efficacy of these agents has been the subject of numerous investigations. For instance, Prasant et al. conducted a randomised, double-blinded study comparing preoperative nebulization with ketamine and 4% lignocaine. Their findings indicated that ketamine provided superior prophylaxis against moderate-to-severe POST, with significantly lower incidence rates at 6 and 24 hours post-extubation compared to lignocaine [1]. Similarly, Saradas and Padmavathi reported that ketamine nebulization was more effective than saline in reducing both the incidence and severity of POST, highlighting its potential as a first-line preventive measure [6]. In addition to ketamine and lignocaine, other agents such as magnesium sulphate and dexamethasone have been explored for their roles in POST prevention. Magnesium, another NMDA receptor antagonist, exhibits anti-nociceptive and anti-inflammatory effects, making it a viable alternative to ketamine. A systematic review and meta-analysis by Makkar et al. demonstrated the efficacy of topical magnesium in reducing POST incidence, although its comparative performance against ketamine and lignocaine remains less well-established [7]. Dexamethasone, a corticosteroid with potent anti-inflammatory properties, has also shown promise. Kumari et al. found that preoperative dexamethasone nebulization significantly reduced the incidence and severity of POST compared to magnesium sulphate, underscoring its utility in clinical practice [2]. Despite these advances, the choice of agent for preoperative nebulization remains a topic of debate. Factors such as patient-specific considerations, potential side effects, and cost-effectiveness play crucial roles in clinical decision-making. For example, while ketamine is highly effective, its psychotropic side effects may limit its acceptability in certain patient populations. Conversely, lignocaine, though less potent, is well-tolerated and widely available, making it a practical option in resource-limited settings [1, 5]. The Indian context provides additional insights into the application of preoperative nebulization for POST prevention. Studies conducted in India have highlighted the high prevalence of POST and the need for cost-effective, easily implementable interventions. For instance, Sharma and Malav compared the effects of ketamine and magnesium sulphate nebulization in an Indian cohort, finding that ketamine was more effective in reducing POST severity without significant adverse effects [4]. Similarly, Vaidiyanathan et al. evaluated the efficacy of lignocaine and ketamine gargles, providing valuable data on alternative delivery methods for these agents [5]. These findings underscore the importance of tailoring interventions to the specific needs and constraints of the Indian healthcare system. While the existing literature provides robust evidence for the efficacy of preoperative nebulization in POST prevention, several gaps remain. Comparative studies involving larger sample sizes and diverse patient populations are needed to establish definitive guidelines. Additionally, the long-term safety profiles of agents like ketamine and lignocaine require further investigation. The present study aims to address these gaps by directly comparing the efficacy of preoperative nebulization with ketamine, 4% lignocaine, and saline in preventing POST. By evaluating both the primary outcomes and potential side effects, this research seeks to inform clinical practice and optimise patient care.

Definitions and Basic Concepts Related to Postoperative Sore Throat and Nebulization Postoperative sore throat (POST) is a frequent complication following general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation. It is characterised by discomfort or pain in the throat, often accompanied by hoarseness, dysphagia, or coughing. The incidence of POST varies widely, ranging from 21% to 65%, depending on factors such as patient characteristics, intubation technique, and anaesthetic management [1]. Although considered minor, POST can significantly impact patient satisfaction and recovery, necessitating effective preventive strategies [2]. Nebulization is a method of drug delivery that involves converting liquid medication into aerosolised particles, allowing direct application to the respiratory mucosa. This technique has gained popularity in anaesthetic practice due to its ease of administration, patient acceptance, and ability to deliver drugs locally with minimal systemic absorption [1]. Preoperative nebulization has been explored as a prophylactic measure against POST, leveraging the anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties of various pharmacological agents [7]. Ketamine, an N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor antagonist, is widely recognised for its anaesthetic and analgesic properties. In the context of POST prevention, ketamine's anti-inflammatory effects and ability to modulate nociceptive pathways have been demonstrated in several studies [4]. Nebulized ketamine acts locally on the airway mucosa, reducing inflammation and pain without significant systemic side effects [10]. A randomised controlled trial comparing ketamine and magnesium sulphate nebulization found ketamine to be more effective in reducing both the incidence and severity of POST [4]. Lignocaine, a widely used local anaesthetic, exerts its effects by blocking sodium channels, thereby inhibiting nerve conduction. Nebulized lignocaine has been employed to prevent POST due to its ability to reduce mucosal irritation and suppress the cough reflex during intubation [5]. A study comparing lignocaine gargle and ketamine gargle found lignocaine to be less effective in preventing POST, highlighting the potential advantages of ketamine in this setting [5]. However, lignocaine remains a viable option due to its established safety profile and ease of use [8]. Saline, often used as a control in clinical trials, serves as a neutral agent in nebulization studies. While saline lacks pharmacological activity, its use in nebulization may provide hydration to the airway mucosa, potentially reducing irritation caused by intubation [6]. Studies comparing saline with active agents such as ketamine and lignocaine consistently report lower efficacy for saline in preventing POST, underscoring the need for pharmacologically active interventions [6]. The mechanisms underlying POST are multifactorial, involving mechanical trauma from intubation, mucosal irritation, and inflammatory responses. Endotracheal intubation can cause epithelial damage, leading to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines and subsequent oedema and pain [3]. Pharmacological agents used in nebulization aim to mitigate these effects by reducing inflammation, modulating pain pathways, and protecting the mucosa [7]. Ketamine's NMDA receptor antagonism and lignocaine's sodium channel blockade represent distinct yet complementary mechanisms for POST prevention [4, 5]. International studies have extensively explored the efficacy of various nebulized agents for POST prevention. A meta-analysis of topical magnesium found significant reductions in POST incidence, highlighting the role of NMDA receptor antagonists in this context [7]. Similarly, a Bayesian network meta-analysis comparing topical agents, including ketamine and lignocaine, identified ketamine as a superior option for reducing POST severity [9]. These findings align with narrative reviews emphasising the importance of targeted interventions to address the inflammatory and nociceptive components of POST [3]. Indian studies have contributed valuable insights into POST management, particularly regarding the use of nebulized ketamine and lignocaine. A prospective study comparing these agents reported lower POST incidence and severity with ketamine, reinforcing its efficacy in the Indian patient population [1]. Another study examining ketamine and saline nebulization found ketamine to be significantly more effective, further supporting its use in clinical practice [6]. These findings are consistent with international data, suggesting broad applicability across diverse populations [4]. Despite the growing body of evidence, gaps remain in the literature regarding optimal dosing, timing, and combinations of nebulized agents for POST prevention. Few studies have directly compared ketamine, lignocaine, and saline in a single trial, limiting the ability to draw definitive conclusions about their relative efficacy. Additionally, the long-term safety of repeated nebulization and its effects on airway health warrant further investigation [3]. Addressing these gaps is essential to refine clinical guidelines and improve patient outcomes. The present study aims to compare the efficacy of preoperative nebulization with ketamine, 4% lignocaine, and saline in preventing POST. By directly evaluating these agents within a single observational framework, this research seeks to provide clarity on their relative benefits and inform evidence-based anaesthetic practice.









3. Epidemiology and Clinical Burden of Postoperative Sore Throat Postoperative sore throat (POST) is a frequent complication following general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation. Despite being classified as a minor complication, it significantly impacts patient satisfaction and postoperative recovery. The incidence of POST has been reported to range between 21% and 65%, depending on factors such as patient characteristics, surgical duration, and anaesthetic techniques [1]. This variability underscores the multifactorial nature of POST and its clinical relevance in perioperative care. POST arises primarily due to mechanical irritation and inflammation of the pharyngeal and laryngeal mucosa caused by endotracheal intubation. The condition is characterised by symptoms such as throat pain, hoarseness, and dysphagia, which may persist for up to 24–48 hours post-surgery. Although transient, these symptoms can delay recovery, prolong hospital stays, and increase healthcare costs. Studies have highlighted that the severity of POST correlates with the size of the endotracheal tube, cuff pressure, and duration of intubation [3]. Globally, the burden of POST has been extensively studied. A systematic review and meta-analysis by Na Yang et al. (2023) emphasised the high prevalence of POST and identified various risk factors, including female gender, prolonged surgeries, and inadequate lubrication during intubation [3]. Similarly, a meta-analysis by Alan Hsi-Wen Liao et al. (2019) demonstrated that the use of lidocaine lubricants significantly reduces intubation-related complications, including POST, suggesting that preventive strategies can mitigate its impact [8]. In India, the clinical burden of POST mirrors global trends, with studies reporting a high prevalence among patients undergoing general anaesthesia. Krishnanakutty Saradas Asha and Padmavathi Padmavathi (2019) conducted a study comparing ketamine and saline nebulisation, revealing that ketamine significantly reduced the incidence and severity of POST [6]. This finding aligns with international evidence supporting the efficacy of ketamine as a prophylactic agent. Another Indian study by N. V. S. N. Prasant et al. (2021) compared preoperative nebulisation with 4% lignocaine and ketamine, concluding that ketamine provides superior prophylaxis against moderate-to-severe POST [1]. The clinical significance of POST extends beyond patient discomfort. It has been associated with increased opioid consumption due to pain management, thereby raising the risk of opioid-related adverse effects. Furthermore, persistent POST can lead to complications such as vocal cord oedema and laryngospasm, particularly in high-risk populations such as paediatric and elderly patients. These complications necessitate additional interventions, further burdening healthcare systems [3]. Preventive strategies for POST have evolved over the years, with a focus on pharmacological and non-pharmacological interventions. Pharmacological approaches include the use of corticosteroids, magnesium sulphate, and local anaesthetics. For instance, a study by Seema Kumari K et al. (2018) demonstrated that dexamethasone nebulisation effectively reduces the incidence and severity of POST, highlighting its anti-inflammatory properties [2]. Similarly, magnesium sulphate, an N-methyl-D-aspartate receptor antagonist, has shown promise in reducing POST due to its anti-nociceptive and anti-inflammatory effects [7]. Ketamine, another N-methyl-D-aspartate receptor antagonist, has gained attention for its dual analgesic and anti-inflammatory properties. Studies have consistently shown that preoperative nebulisation with ketamine reduces the incidence and severity of POST. For example, Kapil Sharma and Virendra Malav (2023) reported that ketamine nebulisation was more effective than magnesium sulphate in preventing POST, further supporting its role as a prophylactic agent [4]. Lignocaine, a widely used local anaesthetic, has also been investigated for its efficacy in preventing POST. A systematic review by Alan Hsi-Wen Liao et al. (2019) highlighted that lignocaine lubricants reduce intubation-related complications, including POST, by minimising mucosal irritation [8]. However, the comparative efficacy of lignocaine versus ketamine remains a subject of ongoing research. Bhavani Vaidiyanathan et al. (2018) compared lignocaine viscous gargle with ketamine gargle, finding that ketamine was more effective in preventing POST after laryngeal mask airway insertion [5]. Despite these advancements, gaps remain in the understanding of POST's epidemiology and management, particularly in resource-limited settings. Most studies have focused on adult populations, with limited data on paediatric and elderly patients. Additionally, the long-term outcomes of POST and its impact on quality of life are underexplored. In India, there is a need for large-scale, multicentric studies to validate the efficacy of various prophylactic agents and develop standardised guidelines for POST prevention. The present study aims to address these gaps by comparing the efficacy of preoperative nebulisation with ketamine, 4% lignocaine, and saline in preventing POST. By evaluating the incidence, severity, and associated side effects, this study seeks to contribute to the growing body of evidence on effective strategies for POST management, ultimately improving patient outcomes and satisfaction.

Anatomy, Physiology, and Pathophysiology of Postoperative Sore Throat Postoperative sore throat (POST) is a frequent complication following general anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation, with an incidence ranging from 21% to 65% depending on the study population and methodology [1]. Although often considered minor, POST can significantly impact patient satisfaction and recovery. Understanding the anatomical structures, physiological mechanisms, and pathophysiological changes involved is essential for effective prevention and management. The anatomical structures implicated in POST primarily include the pharynx, larynx, and trachea. The pharynx, a muscular tube extending from the base of the skull to the oesophagus, is divided into the nasopharynx, oropharynx, and laryngopharynx. The larynx, located anteriorly to the pharynx, houses the vocal cords and serves as a critical structure for phonation and airway protection. The trachea, a cartilaginous tube, extends from the larynx to the bronchi and is lined with pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium. These structures are richly innervated by sensory fibres from the vagus and glossopharyngeal nerves, making them highly sensitive to mechanical and chemical stimuli [3]. Physiologically, the airway mucosa serves as a barrier against pathogens and irritants, maintaining homeostasis through mucociliary clearance and immune responses. The mucosa is covered by a thin layer of mucus, which traps foreign particles and facilitates their removal via coordinated ciliary movement. Additionally, the submucosa contains glands that secrete mucus and serous fluid, contributing to airway hydration. Any disruption to this delicate balance, such as mechanical trauma or desiccation, can lead to inflammation and discomfort [3]. The pathophysiology of POST involves a combination of mechanical, chemical, and inflammatory factors. Endotracheal intubation is the most common cause, as the insertion and presence of the tube can lead to direct mechanical injury to the mucosa. This trauma disrupts the epithelial barrier, exposing underlying tissues to irritants and pathogens. The cuff of the endotracheal tube can exert pressure on the tracheal wall, leading to ischaemia and subsequent inflammation. Studies have shown that the incidence and severity of POST correlate with the size of the endotracheal tube and the duration of intubation [1, 3]. Chemical factors also play a role, particularly in the context of anaesthetic agents and lubricants. Lidocaine, commonly used as a lubricant, has been shown to reduce the incidence of POST by minimising mucosal irritation and providing local anaesthesia [8]. However, excessive use of certain agents can disrupt the mucosal barrier, exacerbating inflammation. The role of nebulised agents such as ketamine and lignocaine in mitigating these effects has been explored in various studies. Ketamine, an N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor antagonist, exhibits anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties, making it effective in reducing POST severity [1, 6]. Lignocaine, a local anaesthetic, acts by stabilising neuronal membranes and reducing nociceptive signalling, thereby alleviating discomfort [1, 8]. Inflammatory responses are central to the development of POST. Mechanical trauma and chemical irritation trigger the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-6 (IL-6) and tumour necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α). These mediators recruit immune cells to the site of injury, amplifying the inflammatory cascade. The resulting oedema and hyperaemia contribute to the characteristic symptoms of POST, including pain, hoarseness, and dysphagia. Evidence suggests that interventions targeting these inflammatory pathways, such as dexamethasone nebulisation, can effectively reduce the incidence and severity of POST [2, 7]. The role of NMDA receptor antagonists like ketamine in modulating the inflammatory response has been a focus of recent research. Ketamine not only provides analgesia but also inhibits the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines, thereby attenuating the inflammatory response. A study comparing ketamine and lignocaine nebulisation found that ketamine was more effective in preventing moderate-to-severe POST, likely due to its dual anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects [1]. Similarly, magnesium, another NMDA receptor antagonist, has demonstrated efficacy in reducing POST through its anti-nociceptive and anti-inflammatory properties [7, 4]. The anatomical and physiological characteristics of the airway also influence the risk of POST. For instance, patients with pre-existing conditions such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) or allergic rhinitis may have heightened sensitivity to airway irritation. Additionally, factors such as inadequate humidification of inspired gases and prolonged surgical duration can exacerbate mucosal desiccation and inflammation. Strategies to mitigate these risks include the use of humidified anaesthetic gases and minimising intubation time [3]. Indian studies have contributed valuable insights into the pathophysiology and management of POST. A study by Krishnanakutty et al. highlighted the efficacy of ketamine nebulisation in reducing POST incidence compared to saline, emphasising its role in modulating both nociceptive and inflammatory pathways [6]. Another study demonstrated the superiority of lignocaine viscous gargle over ketamine gargle in preventing POST after laryngeal mask airway insertion, suggesting that the method of administration may influence outcomes [5]. These findings underscore the need for tailored interventions based on patient-specific factors and surgical contexts. Despite advancements in understanding the mechanisms underlying POST, several gaps remain. The relative contributions of mechanical versus inflammatory factors are not fully elucidated, and the optimal dosing and timing of prophylactic interventions require further investigation. Additionally, the long-term effects of repeated exposure to nebulised agents such as ketamine and lignocaine are not well understood. Addressing these gaps is crucial for developing evidence-based guidelines for POST prevention. In summary, the anatomy, physiology, and pathophysiology of POST involve a complex interplay of mechanical, chemical, and inflammatory factors. The pharynx, larynx, and trachea are the primary anatomical sites affected, with disruptions to the mucosal barrier and inflammatory responses playing central roles. Interventions targeting these mechanisms, such as nebulised ketamine and lignocaine, have shown promise in reducing POST incidence and severity. However, further research is needed to optimise these strategies and address existing knowledge gaps, providing the rationale for the present study.

The diagnosis of POST primarily relies on patient-reported symptoms, which include throat pain, hoarseness, and dysphagia. These symptoms are often assessed using structured questionnaires or visual analogue scales (VAS). The VAS is a widely used tool in clinical and research settings to quantify the severity of POST. Patients are asked to rate their pain on a scale of 0 to 10, where 0 represents no pain and 10 represents the worst imaginable pain. This method has been employed in numerous studies, including those comparing the efficacy of preoperative nebulisation with ketamine and lignocaine [1, 6]. The VAS provides a simple and reproducible measure of symptom severity, facilitating comparisons across different interventions. In addition to the VAS, other scoring systems have been utilised to assess POST. The Ramsay Sedation Score, although primarily designed to evaluate sedation levels, has been adapted in some studies to assess patient comfort and symptom severity in the postoperative period [1]. This score categorises patients into different levels of sedation and discomfort, providing a semi-quantitative measure of POST severity. While not specific to POST, its inclusion in studies highlights the multifaceted approach to assessing patient outcomes. Several studies have also employed time-based assessments to evaluate the progression of POST symptoms. For instance, the incidence and severity of POST are often recorded at multiple time points, such as immediately after extubation (0 hours), and at 6, 12, and 24 hours postoperatively [1, 2]. This longitudinal approach allows researchers to capture the temporal dynamics of POST, identifying peak symptom periods and the duration of relief provided by different interventions. For example, ketamine nebulisation has been shown to significantly reduce the incidence of moderate-to-severe POST at 6 and 24 hours compared to lignocaine [1]. Objective diagnostic methods, such as laryngoscopic examination, are less commonly used due to their invasive nature and the potential to exacerbate symptoms. However, they can provide valuable insights into the anatomical and physiological changes associated with POST. Laryngoscopic findings, such as erythema, oedema, and vocal cord lesions, have been correlated with symptom severity in some studies [3]. These findings underscore the importance of integrating subjective and objective measures for a comprehensive assessment of POST. The choice of diagnostic tools and scoring systems often depends on the study design and objectives. Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing different interventions for POST prevention frequently employ standardised tools like the VAS to ensure consistency and reliability. For instance, a systematic review and meta-analysis of topical magnesium for POST prevention highlighted the use of VAS as a primary outcome measure in most included studies [7]. Similarly, studies comparing ketamine and magnesium sulphate nebulisation have relied on VAS scores to demonstrate the efficacy of these agents in reducing POST severity [4]. In the Indian context, studies have predominantly focused on the VAS and time-based assessments to evaluate POST. For example, a study comparing ketamine and saline nebulisation reported a significant reduction in POST severity with ketamine, as measured by VAS scores at multiple time points [6]. Another Indian study comparing lignocaine and ketamine gargles also utilised VAS scores to assess symptom severity, highlighting the widespread adoption of this tool in regional research [5]. These studies contribute valuable data to the global understanding of POST while addressing the unique clinical and demographic characteristics of the Indian population. Despite the availability of various diagnostic methods and scoring systems, several gaps remain in the assessment of POST. Most tools rely on subjective patient reports, which can be influenced by individual pain thresholds and communication abilities. Objective measures, such as biomarkers of inflammation or advanced imaging techniques, are rarely employed but could provide additional insights into the pathophysiology of POST. Furthermore, the lack of standardisation in diagnostic criteria and scoring systems across studies complicates the comparison of results and the synthesis of evidence. The present study aims to address some of these gaps by employing standardised diagnostic tools, such as the VAS, to compare the efficacy of preoperative nebulisation with ketamine, 4% lignocaine, and saline in preventing POST. By incorporating time-based assessments and evaluating side effects, this study seeks to provide a comprehensive understanding of the interventions' impact on POST. The findings will contribute to the optimisation of diagnostic and therapeutic strategies for this common postoperative complication.
